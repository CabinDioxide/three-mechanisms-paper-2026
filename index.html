中文政治词汇中合法横向联合的范畴边界
——Universitas 缺失的两千五百年

张笑宇  ·  工作论文  ·  2026 年 4 月

The Categorical Boundary of Legitimate Horizontal Association in Chinese Political Vocabulary
— Twenty-five Centuries of Universitas Absence

Zhang Xiaoyu  ·  Working Paper  ·  April 2026
摘 要

1894 年 11 月，孙中山在檀香山办了第一个反清政治组织，叫"兴中会"。后来戊戌变法的"保国会"、1903 年的"光复会"、1905 年东京的"同盟会"——晚清改革派一次次组织政治团体，都用"会"。直到 1912 年宋教仁把同盟会改成国民党，"党"字才被正式推上去；但他第二年就被刺杀，这套用"党"组织政治力量的实验，在中国第一次尝试就失败了。

为什么用"会"不用"党"？因为"会"在中文里有祭祀（社稷之会）、契约（盟会）、聚会（讲会）的意思，没有坏的味道；而"党"从《论语》"群而不党"开始，两千五百年里一直跟"私"、"阿比"、"偏私"这些词绑在一起。改革者不是不能组织，是不能用"党"这个字。

本文要回答的是：中文里到底有哪些词可以用来指代合法的横向政治联合？哪些被允许、哪些被骂、哪些会摇摆？跨越王朝看下去，分布怎么变？

做法是：用 1.04 亿字的古籍语料库（先秦到晚清），训练十个不同时段的词向量模型，对约 130 个候选词扫描每个时段的"极性"——也就是这个词在那个时代的话语里是被当成正面还是负面。结果分四点。

一、所有词分成三组。 一组始终被当成正面——友谊、道德身份、伦理原则、学术活动。一组来回摇摆——典型是集体决策类的词，"公议"在汉代特别正面（极性 +0.227），唐代降到负（−0.106），宋代又回升（+0.181）。一组始终被当成负面——党、群、徒、辈、类、侪……这组词的共同点是"成员被吞没在集合里"，十王朝平均都在 −0.10 左右。

二、不是所有"集体性词汇"都被禁。 誓约一类（盟、誓、契、约、结义）能用，共同体一类（社、乡党、宗族、邻里）也能用。被禁的只有"个体被吞没的法人"那一类——党、侪、徒、侣、辈、类、群、众、伍、派、团全部负化。只有一个字逃出来了——"会"，平均极性 +0.049。它后来承担了几乎所有合法横向组织的命名功能（议会、国会、学会、兴中会、同盟会、光复会）。

三、这个分布正好对应中世纪欧洲的三种法律术语。 Berman（1983）和 Tierney（1955）这两位欧洲法律史家指出过：中世纪欧洲发展出三种合法的集体性词汇——conjuratio（誓约联合）、communitas（共同体）、universitas（法人）。中文有前两种——"盟""誓"是 conjuratio，"宗族""乡党"是 communitas——但作为"合法政治主体"的 universitas（也就是法人这一类）在中文里没有。这就是"中国为何没发展出宪政"的精确答案。

四、晚明东林一案像一场完美的自然实验。 同一群人——顾宪成、高攀龙等几十位儒家学者——自称"东林书院"时极性是 +0.054；被对手叫"东林党"时变成 −0.108。只换一个名字，差距 0.184，几乎等于"君子"和"小人"两个词之间的全部距离。再看晚清：1898 到 1911 年间，从日语借进来的西方政治词分两路——"议会"、"国会"、"学会"都顺利落地（极性接近 +0.05），但"政党"、"民党"始终被骂（−0.077 和 −0.103）。"会"系通过，"党"系封锁，朋党之禁最后一次显形就是这道分轨。

把这些发现合到一起，就是一个三机制框架：领土法人组织、横向联合的话语合法性（本文重点）、血缘共同体基底的解体。它在中国语境下精化了 Acemoglu & Robinson（2019）的"窄走廊"模型。新加坡是目前唯一稳定的"自由华人"政体，原因就是同时改造了这三个机制。

关键词：中国政治词汇 · 合法横向联合 · 词向量 · 计算概念史 · 比较制度分析 · Universitas

1. 引言

1.1 一个尖锐的问题

1894 年 11 月 24 日，孙中山在檀香山创立兴中会。"兴中会"三字今天读来平平无奇，但是一次精心选择的命名。在十九世纪末的中文政治词汇里，"会"是一个特殊的字——它承载祭祀（社稷之会）、契约（盟会）、聚会（讲会）的含义，没有朋比的污名。它不是"党"，不是"盟"（晚清"盟"已大幅负化），不是"社"（晚明复社的失败证明此字易被反向利用）。在汉语政治话语中，"会"几乎是仅存的安全岛。

这种选词上的回避，不是孙中山一个人的语感。光绪帝在 1898 年 6 月 11 日颁布"明定国是"诏，宣告维新变法——整篇诏书几百字，没有"政党"二字。康有为、梁启超在戊戌变法期间组织的团体，叫"保国会"——又是"会"。1903 年蔡元培、章太炎等成立"光复会"。1905 年东京中国留学生组织反清同盟，叫"同盟会"——"同盟"两字本身已是一种语义自卫，但仍要用"会"字压尾。1912 年宋教仁把同盟会改组为国民党，必须用"国"字给"党"做合法性背书；第二年宋教仁被刺，这次实验当场失败。

每一次选词背后是同一个问题：为什么"党"在中文里始终是攻击性词汇而"会"是合法的？为什么改革者每次想组织一个超越血缘的、可持续的、能和最高权威对话的政治力量，都要在用词上小心翼翼地绕开"党"？

这个问题比表面看起来要尖。它问的不是"为什么中国落后"——1750 年中国和欧洲在收入、城市化、商业复杂度上大体相当（Wong 1997；Pomeranz 2000）。也不是"为什么中国没有民主"——民主是相当晚近的政治形态，需要的不只是制度，还有特定的话语土壤。这个问题问的是：为什么在多次具备物质条件、官僚理性、商业繁荣的窗口期里，中国都没有出现一个能约束最高权威、由非血缘成员组成的合法政治主体？

要回答这个问题，必须进入中文政治词汇的内部去看。但只盯一个字（比如"党"）或一个时段（比如北宋党争）不够——一个字的好坏，总是由它周围那一堆字共同决定的。"党"为什么是攻击性词汇？只看"党"字本身找不出答案，要把它放回"会""社""盟""派""徒""群""学"这些横向集合候选词的关系里看。本文因此把所有这些候选词一次全扫描，看哪些被允许、哪些被禁、哪些会摇摆。孙中山为什么选"会"不选"党"的细节，由此被放回两千五百年的语义场演化里来理解。

1.2 三类既有解释及其边界

"中国为何没发展出宪政"这个问题，现有研究主要有三类回答。每一类都做了很多工作，但每一类都有它解释不到的地方。

第一类是物质条件解释——商业、技术、军事、地理（Pomeranz 2000；Allen 2009；Mokyr 1990）。但 Wong（1997）和 Pomeranz（2000）的"加州学派"已经把这条路堵上了：到 1750 年，中国和欧洲在多项关键指标上大体相当。物质条件差不多，政治走向却分叉，那答案显然在别处。

第二类是政治-制度解释，关注帝国主权结构、官僚组织形态、商人-军事精英联盟的缺席（Greif 2006；Mokyr & Tabellini 2023；Acemoglu & Robinson 2019；Stasavage 2020）。最新一步是 Mokyr & Tabellini 的"领土法人 vs. 血缘宗族"：欧洲的领土法人（行会、特许城市、议会）有产权、有议价能力，可以作为对手方跟中央国家谈判；中国的宗族做不到这件事——它们能补充国家，但没法约束国家。这个解释有力，但有两个问题。一是 Ogilvie（2019）已经证明欧洲行会主要是寻租组织——"能议价"和"能促进发展"不是一回事，要区分开。二是更重要的：这个解释只到组织形态为止，没追到话语条件。组织形态的缺失和话语合法性的缺失，其实是同一件事的两面——前者发生在土地和制度上，后者发生在词汇和语义上。两者互为因果。

第三类是话语-制度解释，最完整的版本是 Ari Levine（2005, 2008）。他细读北宋后期政治文本，发现一件事：北宋政治词汇本身就让官僚精英之间的合法横向联合无法成立。庆历改革派曾经想通过区分"君子之朋"和"小人之党"来重新定义朋党，但很快失败；接下来一连串政治周期没有一次走向制度性容纳，全都升级成政治清洗。

Levine 的细读至今仍是对中国政治话语最敏锐的，覆盖 1067 到 1104 年关键党争时段。但他的工作留下三个未答的问题：话语之禁是否超出北宋？除了"朋党"之外，中文里还有没有别的合法横向联合范畴？这些范畴和"朋党"的边界画在哪？要回答这三个问题，必须跨越更长时段、覆盖更多候选词，并用可定量检验的方法——这就是本文的设计。

1.3 一个跨越多重学术传统的问题

朋党之禁这一现象已经被多个学术传统从不同角度识别过。传统训诂（何晏、皇侃、朱熹、刘宝楠）一致将"党"训为"私助、阿比、偏私"。民国-当代汉学（陈寅恪、川胜义雄、宇都宫清吉、余英时）从社会集团竞争、清流-乡论、豪族经济、士之群体自觉四个层面解释党锢与历代党争。当代汉学话语-政治分析（Peter Bol、Hilde De Weerdt、邓小南）追溯唐宋"士"概念转型、南宋话语网络、北宋"祖宗之法"作为话语规范。晚明东林研究（Hucker、小野和子、Dardess）记录了 movement / faction 的自他不对称命名。比较制度分析（Greif、Rubin、Stasavage、Acemoglu-Robinson）提供跨文明的比较框架。计算概念史（Hamilton、Garg、Kozlowski、Rodman）建立词向量方法的工具箱。

本文把这些侧面放进同一个分析框架——通过对中文政治词汇的全谱系扫描，把六支学术传统的质性观察纳入一个可定量检验的语义场域演化叙事。

1.4 三个发现及一个新论断

本文用 Mikolov 等（2013）的词向量方法，在一个跨越先秦到晚清、规模 1.04 亿字的古典中文语料库上独立训练十个分时段模型。然后对约 130 个候选词（按七层语义结构组织）做全谱系扫描，算出每个词在每个时段的"极性"——也就是被当成正面还是负面。

发现一：中文政治词汇并不缺集体性词汇，它精确地缺一种特定范畴

扫描下来，第 1 到第 6 层、加上第 7a 层（共同体）的候选词，在十个王朝中要么始终合法、要么时序振荡——友谊（友 +0.151）、道德（贤 +0.222、君子 +0.082）、伦理（仁 +0.262、公义 +0.327）、学术（讲学 +0.066、书院 +0.055）、誓约（盟 +0.037、结义 +0.106）、集体决策（公议汉代峰值 +0.232）、共同体（宗族 +0.034、祭社 +0.052）。唯独第 7b 层——也就是"个体被吞没在集合里"那一类候选字根——全部负化。

发现二：禁绝的范畴对应 Berman-Tierney 三重区分中的 universitas

这套分布正好对应 Berman（1983）和 Tierney（1955）所讲的中世纪欧洲三种合法化术语：

Conjuratio
誓约联合
盟、誓、契、约
结义、会盟
✓ 合法
Communitas
共同体
社、乡党、宗族
邻里、州里
≈ 中性偏正
Universitas
法人 / 个体被吞没的合法集合
党、群、徒、辈、类、侪
✗ 一致禁绝

中文里没有发展出对应 universitas 的合法范畴。朋党之禁的真正形态就在这里：它不是泛泛地禁所有"集体性"，而是禁一种特定的集体性——能作为合法政治主体行动、成员被吞没在整体里、由这个整体替成员发言的那种集合。

发现三：禁的不是字本身，而是"在作为合法政治主体行动"这种语义结构

同一个字根换一种搭配，命运就完全不一样。"会"单字 +0.049 合法，跟具体政治内容搭配后分两路：议会（+0.042）、国会（+0.057）、保国会（+0.073）合法；复社（晚明 −0.137）、学派（晚清 −0.109）被骂。要破朋党之禁不能靠造一个新字——新字一旦被读成承载 universitas 的语义结构，就被自动拉进禁地。

新论断

中国没发展出宪政，并不是因为中文话语缺集体性词汇——中文有完整的 conjuratio（盟、誓、契、约）、communitas（社、乡党、宗族）、集体审议（公议、公论、廷议）、学术结合（讲会、书院）这些合法范畴。中国精确缺的，是 universitas 这一类——作为合法政治主体的"无差别成员之集合"。这一缺失从先秦《论语》"群而不党"开始，跨过党锢之祸的司法制度化、北宋庆历的话语战、晚明东林的命名战、晚清"政党"的引入，两千五百年里稳定运作。

2. 理论框架

2.1 Levine 假说及其当代接受

Ari Levine 在《同语异义：北宋晚期的派系冲突》（2008，建立在 Levine 2005 之上）里对北宋政治话语提出三个层层嵌套的主张。第一，"朋党"这个词在北宋政治实践里的核心功能不是描述，而是攻击——每一个政治集团都用它指控对手，同时坚决否认自己有派系性质。这种"攻人则用、自指则讳"的不对称用法，使"朋党"没办法演变成一个中性的描述性范畴。第二，范仲淹和欧阳修在庆历改革期（1043–44）尝试通过区分"君子之朋"和"小人之党"为合法横向联合开辟话语空间，但这次改造没成功。第三，皇权在整个体系里扮演终极仲裁者——任何萌芽中的横向联合，皇帝随时可以撤回它的合法性。

本文跟 Levine 的关系是把他的工作延伸出去，方向有三个：把分析时段从北宋三十七年扩展到先秦至晚清两千五百年；把质性的修辞分析转化为可定量检验的词向量观测；把"朋党"这一个词放回它所在的整个语义场，看清楚禁绝的精确边界（哪些字根被禁、哪些放过、边界在哪）。Levine 说清楚了北宋"朋党"作为攻击工具的不对称用法；本文要进一步看清这种用法运作的精确范畴边界，以及它在更长时段里的稳定形态。

2.2 一个关键的概念资源：Berman-Tierney 三重区分

要看清中国到底缺了什么，得借一个欧洲法律史的工具。这个工具在中文研究里用得不多，但对本文论证很关键。Harold Berman 在《法律与革命：西方法律传统的形成》（1983）里系统论证过：12 到 13 世纪欧洲的"教皇革命"，以及随之而来的法律科学，是建立在三个互相独立但又互相支撑的合法化术语之上的——universitas、communitas、conjuratio。Brian Tierney《公会议运动思想的基础》（1955）则在更早的时段里追溯了 universitas 这个概念在中世纪教会法学家手里是怎么发展起来的。这两本书加在一起确立了一件事：欧洲发展出来的不是"一种"集体性词汇，而是三种互相分工的集体性范畴。

Universitas（法人） 是其中最关键的。它的拉丁文字面意思是"整全性"，但在中世纪法学手里获得了一个精确的技术含义：一个由无差别成员组成、作为单一法律主体行动的集合。它的核心特征是个体被吞没——一个人一旦加入 universitas，他作为这个法人的成员去行动时，不再用自己的名义，而用整体的名义。法人因此可以拥有产权、签合同、起诉、被诉——它是一个人造的法律人格。中世纪欧洲的大学、城市、行会、修道会，以及后来的领土法人，都属于 universitas。

Communitas（共同体） 是另一种，指地缘性、仪式性、内部有差序的集合。中世纪欧洲的村落、教区、行省、民族（natio）都是 communitas。它跟 universitas 的关键区别在于：communitas 不要求成员被吞没——成员保留自己的差异、地位、家族归属。

Conjuratio（誓约联合） 是第三种，指契约性、誓约性、可以解除的集合。中世纪欧洲城市的诞生大多是从 conjuratio 起步的：一群市民互相宣誓、订契约、共同付保证金，结成一个互相保护、互相约束的联合。十字军、商业联盟、骑士团都属于这一类。

这三种范畴在中世纪欧洲是叠加的，不是互斥的——一个具体的城市可能同时是 universitas civium（法人）、communitas（地缘共同体）和 conjuratio（市民誓约联合）。只有三者同时存在，才能形成一个可持续的、能约束最高权威、由非血缘成员构成的合法政治主体。

§4 的全谱系扫描会显示：中文里发展出了 conjuratio 和 communitas 的合法范畴——盟、誓、契、约、结义、会盟承担 conjuratio 的功能；社、乡党、宗族、祭社承担 communitas 的功能——但作为"个体被吞没的合法集合"的 universitas，党、群、徒、辈、类、侪这些候选字根全部被系统地污名化。

2.4 三个机制——A-R 框架的中国化精化

Acemoglu & Robinson 在《窄走廊》（2019）里提出一个两因素模型：进入"窄走廊"（国家和社会平衡的稳定状态）需要国家和社会同时强大，通过"红女王效应"互相制衡。他们把中国定位为"专制利维坦"。比批评 A-R 更重要的，是要看到：A-R 把"社会"当作单一一极，这在中国问题上不够，需要把它再拆开。本文把"社会"这一极拆成三个独立机制。

机制一：领土法人组织（universitas）的存在。 这是 Mokyr-Tabellini（2023）识别的核心变量。欧洲的领土法人——行会、特许城市、议会、三级会议——有产权也有议价能力，能作为法律对手方跟中央国家谈判。中国的宗族在结构上做不到这件事。Ogilvie（2019）对 M-T 论提出根本性挑战：欧洲行会主要是寻租组织。但 Ogilvie 的批评是规范性的（寻租是坏事），不是结构性的（领土法人不存在）——她描述的"行会-精英寻租联盟"本身就是一个议价结构，跟 M-T 的核心论断方向一致。

机制二：横向联合的话语合法性（universitas 范畴的存在）。 这是本文要从其他两个机制里定量隔离出来的核心变量。精确表述是：在中文话语里，对应 universitas 的所有候选字根（党、群、徒、辈、类、侪）从先秦到晚清两千五百年间一直被系统地负面化。

机制三：血缘共同体的解体。 这是 Ōtsuka（1966）识别的、被中国学界长期忽视的变量。亚洲共同体（kyōdōtai）是以血缘为基础的生产单位，按互惠逻辑而不是市场逻辑组织土地、劳动和交换。资本主义之所以能在中世纪欧洲城市萌芽，关键在于教会从 4 到 7 世纪开始持续禁止表亲婚姻，主动把血缘共同体打断了；亚洲社会从来没经历过这一断裂。

这三个机制在分析上是独立的——每一个原则上都可以单独存在或缺失——但在经验上互相强化。只有三个机制同时具备，才能产生稳定的宪政发展——这是 §6 跨文明比较要论证的。

2.5 宪政概念的精确定义

本文采用 Stasavage（2020）和 Acemoglu & Robinson（2019）的弱约束性定义，把"宪政"分成三个递进层次。层次 A（最低门槛）：存在制度化的、横贯性的非国家实体，作为最高权威决策的合法对手方。层次 B（早期民主，Stasavage 意义）：这些对手方在具体决策上有积极同意权，不只是被咨询。层次 C（现代民主）：层次 B 的同意权被规范化为代议制度，配以定期选举和非命令式授权。

中国的问题是在层次 A 就已经失败——这是本文的核心定位。要解释的不是"中国为何没发展出现代代议制"（这个问法已经预设了层次 B 已具备），而是"中国为何没发展出最低门槛的横贯性合法政治主体"。§4.4 会证明：汉代的"公议"在词向量空间里具有强烈正极性（西汉 +0.165、东汉 +0.232——超过北宋高峰 +0.181）。汉代是有过一个层次 B 的合法话语空间的——这个空间在唐代被压下去，宋代被重新激活，但从未上升到层次 A。也就是说，L6（公议）的合法性在不同朝代来回出现过，但作为合法政治主体的 universitas 始终没有。

3.2 极性得分

本文沿用计算概念史成熟的极性得分方法（Hamilton et al. 2016；Garg et al. 2018；Kozlowski et al. 2019），将词项 w 的极性定义为：

$$\text{Polarity}(w) = \frac{1}{|A_+|}\sum_{a \in A_+} \cos(w, a) - \frac{1}{|A_-|}\sum_{a \in A_-} \cos(w, a)$$

主锚词集：$A_+ = \{$君, 忠, 賢, 仁, 義, 公$\}$；$A_- = \{$小, 奸, 佞, 邪, 私$\}$。这一公式与 Garg 等（2018）的 relative norm difference 在功能上等价。本文同时采用四个独立锚词族（道德、法律、朝廷-私人、忠诚）平行计算，结果在性质上完全一致。

这一公式背后的几何直觉值得花一点篇幅说明，因为它决定后续所有发现的可读性。词向量训练把每个字嵌入一个 200 维空间——出现在相似语境中的字被放到空间中相近的位置。极性得分实际上在问：这个字距离"正向锚词集"有多近、距离"负向锚词集"有多远？

图 A · 极性得分的几何示意
词向量空间的概念示意。正锚词集（绿点：君、忠、贤、仁、义、公）形成一个集群，负锚词集（红点：小、奸、佞、邪、私）形成另一个集群。任何一个目标词的"极性"等于它到正锚词集中心的距离 − 它到负锚词集中心的距离。"会"靠近正锚词集（极性为正），"党"靠近负锚词集（极性为负）。

3.5 义项消歧

古典中文中"党"字至少有三个独立义项：行政单位（"五百家为党"）、血缘宗族（"父党、母党"）、派系结伙（"群而不党""阿党"）。本文采用基于上下文规则的义项消歧——对每个"党"字位置扫描 ±15 字上下文，分类为派系、血缘、行政、未明四类，重新分词训练 word2vec。§4 报告：派系义的 sim(党, 私) 上升到 +0.463，行政义和血缘义接近 0——禁绝精确针对派系义。

4.3 三个稳定的合法范畴：L1–L3 与 L5、L7a 的内部结构

L1–L2–L3 的稳定合法。友（μ = +0.151）、贤（+0.222）、仁（+0.262）、义（+0.222）、忠（+0.233）、公义（+0.327）。这些词从先秦到晚清都保持强烈正向极性，承担的是"个人作为道德主体"的合法话语功能，没有一个用来指"集体作为政治主体"。集体那一层要看 L4 以上。

L4 学术活动的合法。学（+0.083）、讲学（+0.066）、讲会（+0.064）、道学（+0.027）、理学（+0.072）、书院（+0.055）——L4 的候选词全部合法。这是中文话语里允许"非血缘成员组织起来共同行动"的最强合法形式，但有一个前提：这种组织得被认作是学术性的，不是政治性的。一旦"X 学派"被读出政治意涵（比如晚清"学派" −0.109），它就被拉进禁地。

L5 Conjuratio 的中性偏正。盟（+0.037）、誓（+0.034）、契（+0.010）、结义（+0.106）、会盟（+0.058）。其中结义（μ = +0.106）尤其高——这是中文里"非血缘个体相互结约"最干净的合法形式。三国时代关羽、刘备、张飞"桃园结义"的话语遗产，就是中文 conjuratio 最具代表性的形态。

L7a Communitas 的中性偏正。社（+0.001）、宗族（+0.034）、州里（+0.053）、祭社（+0.052）、社稷（+0.043）。这一组是中文里"地缘 + 血缘 + 仪式"的合法集合形式，承担 communitas 的功能——给"我们是谁"提供归属感，但不要求成员被吞没为单一政治主体。

L1–L3、L5、L7a 加在一起，是中文话语里合法集合性词汇的整个库存。这个库存比一般人想象的要丰富——覆盖友谊、道德身份、伦理原则、学术活动、誓约联合、共同体五个层级。中文里并不缺"非血缘成员组织起来"的合法形式。那为什么中国还是没发展出宪政？答案不在这五层——它们都合法。答案必须在剩下的两层：L6（集体决策）和 L7b（universitas）。

图 3 · "公议"在十王朝间的极性时序
汉代是合法化高峰（+0.165 / +0.232，超过宋代）；唐代是唯一负值低谷（−0.138）；宋代是对汉代先例的复兴而非发明。

第一，汉代是合法化的高峰。东汉极性 +0.232——是十个时段里最高的——超过北宋高峰 +0.181。汉代的"公议"处在稳定的正向语义场里，跟皇权下的集体咨议直接相关，典型例子就是《汉书》《后汉书》里大量记载的廷议、朝议、博士议。

这一发现直接反驳 Stasavage（2020）。他在书里说："as far back as we have historical writing in China, we don't see traditions or histories of council governance or early democracy"（中国历史一开始就看不到议事或早期民主的传统）。本文的证据显示这话不准确。汉代曾经有过 Stasavage 意义上的层次 B 合法话语空间——只是从来没上升到层次 A（合法政治主体），因为 L7b（universitas）一直被禁。

第二，唐代的低谷值得注意。唐代极性 −0.138——是整个时序里唯一的负值。这反映了唐代政治的特殊结构：贵族-军事性的政治体让儒家-官僚的集体审议失去了中心地位。Peter Bol（1992）的"斯文"论为这件事提供了机制解释：唐代的"士"是世袭门阀，宋代的"士"才转型为科举官僚。

第三，宋代是对汉代先例的复兴。北宋极性回升到 +0.181——这是重新合法化，不是首次合法化。余英时《朱熹的历史世界》（2003）所讲的"国是法度化"，可以重读为对汉代集体审议词汇的恢复运动。

这个时序让简单的"朋党之禁"叙事变得复杂，也让它更精确：L6（集体决策）从来没被永久锁死，它会跟着王朝的政治哲学取向，反复经历合法化和压制的循环；L7b（universitas）这一类——也就是朋党之禁的真正核心对象——在所有十个时段都呈现惊人均匀的负面化。"L6 可变、L7b 不变"这个非对称结构，是朋党之禁最精确的形态学描述。

4.5 一个一致禁绝的范畴：L7b Universitas

图 4 · 第 7b 层（Universitas）单字按禁绝程度排序
"党"是最强烈被禁绝的字（μ = −0.095），"会"是唯一逃脱禁绝的字（μ = +0.049）——它承担了几乎全部"合法横向联合"的功能（议会、国会、学会、保国会、兴中会、同盟会、光复会）。

为什么这样分群？这一组字的语义内核是什么？ 被禁的这组字共享一个特定语义内核——成员被吞没在一个无差别集合里：

  • 党：私下结伙（朋比为奸，"群而不党"）
  • 群：与众不分（"群居终日言不及义"）
  • 徒：被卷入潮流（一时之徒、徒党）
  • 辈：被同化为某代（吾辈之耻）
  • 类：被划归一物（不类君子）
  • 侪：被列入同伦（侪辈相近）

这一组字的共同点是取消个体的差异性——把成员吞没在一个无差别的集合身份里。这正好对应 universitas 的核心特征。

逃脱的"会"。"会"指的是契约性、可见、成员个体留存的集合——会议（具体、有边界的事件）、会盟（明确订立的契约）、聚会（临时、可解散的聚拢）。"会"的成员不是被吞没——他们以个体身份参加，会议结束就回到自己原来的身份。这是 conjuratio 的内核，不是 universitas 的。正因为这个语义区分，"会"承担了中文话语里"非血缘成员合法组织"的几乎全部功能。

4.7 党锢之祸：禁绝的司法制度化

"党锢"作为复合词在西汉语料中出现 0 次，在东汉语料中出现 39 次——一个清晰的语义铸造事件。更关键的是"党人"在东汉语料中的语义近邻：诉（0.42）、讼（0.38）、冤（0.36）、誣（0.34）、劾（0.32）、讞（0.30）——全部指向司法迫害的具体动作。这构成一个完整的司法迫害语义场。

图 5 · "党"字跨王朝极性时序
"党"字的极性在从先秦到晚清的每一个时段都为负。十朝均值 −0.103，标准差 0.043，无统计显著趋势。先秦极性已牢固为负——比东汉党锢之祸早约七百年。

党锢之祸由此可以被重新定位：它不是负面化的起源（负面化比它早七百年），而是司法制度化的关键节点——把一个早就存在的语义负载，编码进了正式的法律-行政实践，由此延续 1,700 年。

具体迫害档案。第一次党锢（166 年，桓帝时）：宦官集团利用术士张成的弟子牢修上书，诬告李膺等人"养太学游士，交结诸郡生徒，更相驱驰，共为部党，诽讪朝廷"。指控的关键在"共为部党"——"部党"这个新铸造的复合词，把士人之间的师生友朋关系，重新框定为有组织的政治结社。第二次党锢（169 年）："钩党"机制达到极致——以一人定罪，株连其门生、故吏、父子、兄弟，最终扩展到五族。这是中国法律史上最早、有据可查的"结社罪"判例之一——核心机制不是惩罚具体的犯罪行为，而是惩罚"作为某一集合的成员"这种身份。

4.10 东林命名实验：合法 vs. 禁绝在同一群人身上的转换

晚明 1604 到 1626 年间，顾宪成、高攀龙、邹元标等核心儒家学者群体在三种命名下被指代——"东林书院""东林""东林党"。行动者、活动、制度依托在三种命名下完全相同。唯一变化的变量是命名。

图 7 · 东林命名实验（晚明语料）
从自称"东林书院"的 +0.054，到敌方命名"东林党"的 −0.108，差距 0.184——几乎相当于晚明语料中"君子"（+0.131）与"小人"（−0.115）之间的整个跨度。

命名带来的语义近邻变化更尖锐。东林自称"书院"时，最近邻是"书院"和"攀（攀龙）"——是个学术性的语义邻居。被敌人叫"东林党"时，最近邻变成"珰（宦官帽）"、"党人"、"党"、"阉"。晚明语料把"东林党"放在比"君子"更接近"阉党"的位置：sim(东林党, 阉党) = +0.240，sim(东林党, 君子) = +0.038。

和既有学术传统的对话。这个发现精确印证了三个学术传统的论断：Hucker（1957）的 movement / faction 区分；小野和子（1962）论证的"自他不对称命名"——顾宪成、高攀龙等核心成员从未自称"东林党"，"东林党"作为政治标签第一次大规模出现是在万历三十九年（1611）李三才一案；Dardess（2002）记录的"东林党"标签膨胀化（从核心几十人扩展到 309 名黑名单）。本文是把这一跨越 60 年、中日英三大学术传统的质性命题，第一次提升为可被统计观测的事实。

微观文本证据：阮大铖、王绍徽《东林点将录》（1625）。这份文本进呈魏忠贤后作为罢免和迫害名单，把当时被认定为"东林党"的 109 名官员一一对应到《水浒传》一百零八将（李三才 = 及时雨宋江、叶向高 = 玉麒麟卢俊义、赵南星 = 智多星吴用、高攀龙 = 天魁星孙立、杨涟 = 天罡星朱仝……）。每一位使用《点将录》的官员、御史、经筵官，都在语料里产生新的"东林党 + 贼/盗/逆/凶"共现——这就是 sim(东林党, 阉党) = +0.240 这个数字背后的微观文本生产机制。

4.11 晚清的双轨命运：会系合法 / 党系封锁

1898 到 1911 年间从日语借进来的西方政治词，分成了两条命运截然不同的轨道。

图 8 · 晚清新引入横向联合词的双轨命运
"会"系（议会、国会、保国会）合法化、"党"系（政党、民党）封锁——禁绝的精确形态学在西方冲击下的最后一次显现。

朋党之禁并没有把所有西方话语都挡掉——它精确地只挡了"党"系字根。会系（conjuratio）通过，党系（universitas）封锁。晚清的失败不是因为没有新词——"议会"已经合法化了，"国会"也已经合法化了。失败的是作为合法政治主体的 universitas。直到 1911 年清朝崩溃，党系字根仍然在禁地里。

5. 跨王朝持续性的话语动力学

§4 的发现归纳起来是这样的：中文政治词汇并不缺集体性词汇，但精确地缺了 universitas 这一类。两千五百年里，这种缺失以惊人的稳定性运作，跨越多次外族征服和重大制度断裂。实证证据只能告诉我们"发生了什么"，没法告诉我们"为什么它能持续这么久"。本章要回答的是后一个问题。

5.1 两次失败的并置：北宋 1043 与晚明 1620 年代

最直接的证据来自两场相隔 580 年的话语战。它们用同一份话语武器、面对同一类对手，最后都以同一种方式失败。

北宋庆历改革：1043 年。欧阳修在 1044 年写了《朋党论》——这是中国政治思想史上的一个关键文本。论证的策略是词汇切割："君子与君子以同道为朋，小人与小人以同利为朋……然臣谓小人无朋，惟君子则有之。"切下来之后，"君子之朋"拿到了合法性，"小人之党"被定为非法。但这套论证为什么失败？三层原因。一是话语层不自洽——欧阳修不得不用"伪朋"、"真朋"的二分，这本身就预设了"朋党"作为一个连续的负面范畴是存在的。二是政治效力上失败——《朋党论》是回击而不是先发，是在防御位置上做话语自卫，没办法重置游戏规则。三是历史例证可以被反过来用——欧阳修举舜、周武王作正面例证，敌人完全可以反过来举纣、汉献帝、唐昭宗。庆历改革在 1044 年底被否定，范仲淹、富弼、欧阳修被迫离朝。

晚明东林：1620 年代。东林讲学者面对阉党指控时，又一次援引欧阳修《朋党论》。小野和子《明季党社考》明确记录了顾宪成、高攀龙等人在政治论辩里重新使用了"君子之朋 / 小人之党"这个二分。他们用的是 580 年前的同一份话语武器。结果完全一样。1625 年东林六君子在狱中被打死；1626 年东林后七君子也死。阮大铖《东林点将录》把东林 109 人映射到《水浒》梁山一百零八贼，在符号层面完成了对"君子之朋"叙事的最彻底反转。

跨越 580 年，用同一份话语武器，面对同一类对手，遭遇同样的失败——这不是巧合。这说明 universitas 缺失不是一次性的话语事件，而是一种结构性约束——每一代改革者面前都重复出现，每一次都用同样的方式把同样的话语武器打败。

5.2 川胜义雄反向叙事：输家与赢家的同时性

川胜义雄《六朝贵族制社会研究》（1982）追溯党锢之祸里"清流"在魏晋之际的命运。被压制的清流名士——颍川荀氏、钟氏、陈氏、孔融-郑玄两大集团——通过师友、门生、故吏关系结成网络；他们的后代在曹魏-西晋的转型期成为新的统治阶级；九品中正制的"中正之议"，恰恰建立在他们的"清议"传统之上。换句话说，党锢的输家在几十年后转化为门阀贵族制的赢家。

把川胜的论断和本文的发现并置看：本文论证 universitas 缺失延续两千五百年——这是一个关于语义层失败的负面叙事；川胜论证清流在党锢之后转化为门阀贵族制——这是一个关于社会层成功的正面叙事。两个叙事说的是同一群人、同一个事件，只是从两个不同的层次看到了不同的结果。

这就回到一个深层问题：为什么 universitas 缺失不自我侵蚀？

标准制度变迁理论的"自我侵蚀"机制依赖一个前提：制度必须产生具体的输家，输家才有动力去拆制度。但 universitas 缺失不产生纯粹的输家。被冠以"X 党"之名的人在话语层失败，但在社会层往往是赢家——清流名士变成门阀；东林讲学者的家族变成江南世家；甚至晚清"维新党"的成员后来都成了民国政界的重要人物。输与赢同时存在，让禁绝的"输家"始终没动力去拆这个制度。

再深一层：正是这种输赢同时性，让禁绝可以被所有政治集团反复使用，谁都不抵抗。每一次政治冲突里，攻击方都用"朋党"指控对手；被指控的一方在话语上吃亏，但他的社会地位、家族势力、文化资本不会被这指控彻底摧毁——这些东西通过别的渠道延续下去。下一次冲突的时候，上一次被指控的人可能反过来变成攻击方，又用"朋党"指控新的对手。这种永远的轮换，让禁绝变成一个所有政治参与者反复使用、谁也不愿意拆的工具。用博弈论的话说：禁绝是一个稳定的纳什均衡——没有任何单方有动力先停止使用它。

5.3 De Weerdt 论南宋话语网络：物理存在不充分

Hilde De Weerdt《信息、领土与网络》（2015）系统记录了南宋士人通过笔记、邸报、地图、文集、私史等媒介构建的跨地域话语网络。这个网络的规模和密度都不小——到 11 世纪晚期，宋廷已经不再垄断关于自身和领土的信息生产，士人通过非官方文本流通敏感的政治评论。那为什么南宋没有产生宪政结果？De Weerdt 自己的回答很关键："网络强化了对皇权的劝谏角色，扩大了精英的信息访问，但没有创造横向政治结构或宪政约束。"

这正是本文论断在 De Weerdt 实证基础上的进一步精化：机制 1b 的物理存在不充分——士人网络即便存在，如果它在话语层被定位为"对皇帝的劝谏"，而不是"独立的政治组织"（也就是 universitas），就没办法转化为宪政效力。南宋士人在笔记里可以批评在世人物，但他们不能通过"X 党"或"X 会"组织起来对皇权构成制度性约束——因为话语层把 universitas 给禁了。

5.4 邓小南"祖宗之法"：话语层工具化均衡的同型现象

邓小南《祖宗之法》（2006）的关键发现："祖宗之法"在党争里被双向援引——改革派把它当作"限制时下弊端、整饬政风的理论依据"；只有王安石敢"正面质疑"，宣称"祖宗之法不足守"。但更普遍的情况是改革派和保守派双方都援引"祖宗之法"。把这两件事放一起看，一个共同的话语动力学就浮现出来：只要某个政治词汇在同一群体内部能被双向援引（既可以为我所用，也可以为敌所用），它就会形成一个稳定的工具化均衡，谁也拆不了它。"朋党"是这种均衡的负面版本——双方都用它指控对手，谁都不接受这指控；"祖宗之法"是这种均衡的正面版本——双方都援引它来合法化自己。这件事说明 universitas 缺失不是孤立的"中国特殊性"——它是一种特定话语动力学（双向工具化均衡）的具体表现。

机制 1（领土法人）+ 机制 2（话语合法性）+ 机制 3（共同体解体）—— 只有三个机制同时具备时，才能产生稳定的宪政发展。

6.1 朝鲜李朝：士林书院作为隔离检验

朝鲜李朝在三机制框架的机制 1 上其实比北宋更完备——书院从 1543 年绍修书院开始，到 1741 年增至约 1,000 所，是教科书式的领土法人。但朝鲜没有产生宪政结果。1741 年英祖明确把书院解散定义为"终结朋黨腐败"——他用的是和北宋改革派与反对派在 1043 到 1100 年间所争议的完全相同的两个汉字"朋黨"。儒家话语网格随古典词汇穿过中-韩文化圈，跨越政治体的边界仍然有效。约束跟着话语词汇走，不跟政治体边界走。

6.2 中世纪热那亚：路径 B 的脆弱

中世纪热那亚（1099–1339）由血缘宗族（alberghi）治理，结构上更接近中国宗族而非欧洲领土行会。但 podesteria（1194–1339）通过精巧的机制设计实现了 145 年的商业革命稳定。1339 年后，热那亚崩溃为两百余年的内战，有据可查的革命达三十九次，最终在 1381 年战败威尼斯，长期衰落。脆弱性的深层根源在机制 3：底层共同体未被解体。

中国曾经拥有与 podestà 类似的制度结构——宦官系统。但宦官系统服务于皇权主权，不是跨宗族平衡。儒家话语网格禁止"精英联盟之间的平衡"被概念化为一个可通过制度设计解决的合法政治问题。帝制中国缺少 podestà 制度，不是因为该设计未知——而是因为催生路径 B 的政治逻辑从未作为合法事项结晶。

6.3 伊斯兰世界：与 Universitas 缺失同构的伴随案例

Jared Rubin《统治者、宗教与财富》（2017）论证伊斯兰世界经历了一种跟 universitas 缺失在机制层面几乎完全同构的话语-合法性禁令——ulama（伊斯兰宗教学者群体）通过为苏丹提供合法性，换取议价台上的位置，把商人精英排在这个联盟之外。这套安排自我强化：苏丹一旦依赖 ulama 合法化，转向其他合法性来源就会得罪既得的 ulama。

6.4 新加坡：三机制同时改造的范式

新加坡（1965 年独立至今）在三个机制上都做了系统性的改造。机制 1：威斯敏斯特议会、法定机构（EDB、HDB、CPF）作为独立法人。机制 2：英语公共领域绕开 universitas 缺失——重要政治议题在英语中讨论，"政党"在英语中是 political party，没有"朋党"的污名负载。机制 3：HDB 政策——种族配额（Ethnic Integration Policy 1989）、小户型核心家庭单位、强制性 SERS——有意识拆解血缘共同体。

新加坡的成功不是任何单一机制的成功，而是三个机制同时改造的结果。任何只针对一个机制的部分改革都将不充分。

7. 锁的持续动力学：与制度变迁理论的对话

7.1 标准制度变迁理论的局限

当代制度变迁理论的主流——North（1990）、Greif（2006）、Acemoglu & Robinson（2012）、North, Wallis & Weingast（2009）——共享一个核心论断：所有制度都内含改变自己的种子。但 universitas 缺失拒绝这一框架。它跨越两千五百年没有任何记录在案的关键节点，穿过多次外族征服和重大制度断裂，连 1840 年以后的西方冲击都没能在帝制框架里把它改掉。这种持续性的深度让人困惑。

7.2 Universitas 缺失作为一类不同的制度形式

第一，它不产生具体的输家。物质制度会产生具体的赢家和输家，输家可以动员力量去改变它。但话语合法性禁令不产生具体的输家——每一个政治行动者都能用"朋党"指控对手，同时为自己否认。这个锁因此被普遍工具化，而不是被普遍抵抗。每一次党争都在用这套禁令去攻击对手，每用一次都把禁令加固一次。

第二，它跟共生结构互相强化。机制 2 和机制 3 在中国语境里互相强化。儒家"五伦"（君臣、父子、夫妇、长幼、朋友）精确描绘的就是血缘共同体的伦理；血缘共同体反过来又为儒家伦理提供经验基底。两个机制因此形成一个稳定的耦合均衡。

第三，外部冲击在兼容的话语框架内被吸收。外族征服（蒙、满）让中国社会面对的，仍然是按跟禁令兼容的话语原则组织起来的官僚系统。1840 年以后的西方冲击带来了替代框架，但晚期帝制的回应要么被压制，要么被吸收。真正的转型——民国时代的政党政治、五四运动的话语革命、1949 年以后的党国体制——都发生在帝制话语框架之外。

7.3 改造禁令的两条历史路径

路径 A：整体退出话语框架。新加坡通过英语公共领域走了这条路。成功的条件是：必须有一个已经发达的替代语言系统作为退出选项。

路径 B：国家主导的话语工程。1949 年以后的中国共产党通过自上而下的党国权威，把"党"从负向语义场里拉出来，强加新的正向语义关联。这次改造在 20 世纪 50–80 年代逐步完成。但这种成功是点状的，不是结构性的——"党"作为"中国共产党"的简称获得了正向极性，但其他 L7b 词汇（派系、帮派）仍然是负极性。这种话语工程的成功是垄断性的——它把禁令改造成"一党合法、其他皆非法"的新形态。

7.4 对制度变迁理论的方法论含义

第一，话语合法性禁令需要被纳入比较政治学的核心理论工具箱——它是一类独立的制度形式，不是现有制度变量的"文化背景"。第二，分析话语合法性禁令需要新的方法论工具——词向量分析、网络分析、概念漂移分析。第三，时间尺度需要被重新校准——话语合法性禁令的时间尺度可能比组织-制度路径依赖长一个数量级。第四，"自我侵蚀"作为通用机制需要被限定——所有产生具体输家的组织-制度都内含改变自己的种子，但话语合法性禁令不产生具体输家。

8. 局限与未来方向

8.1 方法论局限

静态 word2vec 的边界。§3.5 通过基于规则的义项消歧应对这一问题。更彻底的做法是使用语境化词向量（GuwenBERT、SikuBERT）。语料选择偏差：晚清语料以《清实录》为主，§4.11 已把论断范围明确限定在"帝制官方框架内"。锚词选择风险：本文采用四个独立锚词族 + 30 字扩展 + 留一交叉验证。内生性问题：§4.11 通过"政党"晚清极性早于政党制度的证据，提供了话语先于制度的实证证据。

8.2 实证扩展

下一研究阶段应做四项工作：(1) 训练独立的朝鲜《李朝实录》语料库，把朝鲜从质性比较案例升级为双案例定量检验；(2) 扩展到越南语料；(3) BERT 语境化向量稳健性；(4) 与金观涛 DSMCTL（1830–1930）相连，形成从先秦到民国的连续叙事。

8.3 跨文明扩展

伊斯兰语料的复制。本文方法可以在阿拉伯语经典语料上重做，定量验证 Rubin 的质性论断。欧洲对照实验。Hofstadter 美国"party"在 1780–1840 年间从非法到合法的转型可以用 EEBO-ECCO 等英语早期现代语料定量复制。

9. 结论

回到本文开头的场景。1894 年孙中山选"兴中会"不选"兴中党"。1898 年光绪帝的"明定国是"诏里没有"政党"二字。1905 年东京中国留学生用的是"同盟会"不是"同盟党"。1912 年宋教仁把"同盟会"改组为"国民党"，但他必须用"国"字给"党"做合法性背书；这次实验在 1913 年宋教仁被刺时当场失败。

本文的回答可以归纳成四点具体发现。一是，中文政治词汇并不缺"集体性词汇"——L1 友谊、L2 道德身份、L3 伦理原则、L4 学术活动、L5 Conjuratio、L7a Communitas 都合法。被精确禁掉的是 L7b Universitas——党、群、徒、辈、类、侪、众这些候选字根。二是，唯一逃出来的字是"会"（μ = +0.049）。它承担了几乎全部合法横向联合的功能。三是，被禁的是"作为合法政治主体行动"这种语义结构，不在具体字符层。四是，晚清的双轨命运给这个论断提供了最干净的现代验证。

中国没发展出宪政，不是因为中文话语缺"集体性词汇"——它精确缺的是一种特定范畴：作为合法政治主体的 universitas。这一缺失从先秦《论语》"群而不党"开始，跨过党锢之祸的司法制度化、北宋庆历的话语战、晚明东林的命名战、晚清"政党"的引入，两千五百年里稳定运作。

回到孙中山 1894 年选"兴中会"那一刻。这个选择既不是偶然的语感，也不是哪一个人的策略——它是两千五百年话语沉积在一个具体改革者身上的结果。"会"二字背后是一整张合法的 conjuratio 语义场——盟、誓、契、约、结义、会盟。"党"二字背后是党锢之祸的司法迫害语义场，是欧阳修《朋党论》的失败回声，是东林六君子在狱中被打死的具体血迹，是"X 党"作为统一非法语义簇的累积压力。孙中山选"会"不选"党"，跟欧阳修在 1044 年选"君子之朋"不选"小人之党"、跟东林讲学者在 1620 年代选"书院"不选"党"、跟复社在 1629 年选"社"不选"党"——是同一件事。每一次都是一个具体改革者面对同一份历史压力的具体应对，每一次都遵循同样的话语生态学：从合法的 conjuratio / communitas 里挑词，避开被禁的 universitas。

Universitas 缺失的具体形态就在这里。它不是抽象的"中国特殊性"，不是泛泛的"中国精神"，也不是某个王朝的政治压制。它是一种具体的、可被精确指出的话语动力学——一种特定语义结构的精确禁绝——在两千五百年里跨越多个具体政治体反复运作。理解它，是理解中国政治演化的必要起点；改造它，是理解中国未来演化的必要起点。

ABSTRACT

In November 1894, Sun Yat-sen founded the first anti-Qing political organization in Honolulu, calling it the "Xingzhonghui" (Revive China Association). The "Baoguohui" of the 1898 Reform, the "Guangfuhui" of 1903, the "Tongmenghui" of 1905 in Tokyo—every late-Qing reform group used "hui" (会, association). Not until 1912, when Song Jiaoren reorganized the Tongmenghui into the Kuomintang (Guomindang), did the character "dang" (党, party) get formally promoted; but Song was assassinated the following year, and this first Chinese attempt to organize political power under "dang" failed at its first try.

Why "hui" and not "dang"? Because "hui" in Chinese carries meanings of sacrifice (社稷之会), covenant (盟会), and gathering (讲会)—none with the stigma of factional collusion. "Dang", from the Analects' "qun er bu dang" (associate without forming factions) onward, has for twenty-five centuries been bound to "si" (private), "e bi" (collusion), and "pian si" (partiality). Reformers are not incapable of organizing—they are incapable of using the character "dang".

This paper asks: which words in Chinese can legitimately denote horizontal political association? Which are sanctioned, which condemned, which oscillate? Tracked across dynasties, how does the distribution evolve?

Method: a corpus of 104 million characters of classical Chinese spanning the pre-Qin to late Qing, ten independently trained period-specific word2vec models, and a full-spectrum scan of about 130 candidate words (organized into seven semantic layers), measuring each word's "polarity" (whether the discourse of its era treats it as positive or negative). Four findings.

I. Words fall into three groups. One group is consistently positive across ten dynasties—friendship, moral identity, ethical principles, scholarly activity. One group oscillates—typically collective-decision words: "gongyi" (public deliberation) is strongly positive in Han (+0.227), falls to negative in Tang (−0.106), recovers in Song (+0.181). One group is consistently negative—dang, qun, tu, bei, lei, chai… united by the trait of "members absorbed into the collective," averaging around −0.10 across ten dynasties.

II. Not all "collective vocabulary" is prohibited. Oath-bond words (meng, shi, qi, yue, jieyi) are usable; community words (she, xiangdang, zongzu, linli) are usable. What is prohibited is exactly the "individual-absorbed-into-corporate-person" type—dang, chai, tu, lü, bei, lei, qun, zhong, wu, pai, tuan, all negative. Only one character escapes: "hui" (会), with mean polarity +0.049. It came to bear nearly all naming functions of legitimate horizontal organizations (yihui/parliament, guohui/national assembly, xuehui/learned society, Xingzhonghui, Tongmenghui, Guangfuhui).

III. This distribution maps precisely onto three medieval European legal categories. Berman (1983) and Tierney (1955), historians of medieval European law, identified that Europe developed three legitimate forms of collective vocabulary: conjuratio (sworn association), communitas (community), universitas (corporation/legal person). Chinese has the first two—"meng" and "shi" are conjuratio, "zongzu" and "xiangdang" are communitas—but the universitas category (corporate legal person as legitimate political subject) does not exist in Chinese. This is the precise answer to "why China never developed constitutionalism."

IV. The Late Ming Donglin case is a near-perfect natural experiment. The same group of people—Gu Xiancheng, Gao Panlong, and a few dozen Confucian scholars—are at polarity +0.054 when they call themselves "Donglin Academy" (Donglin Shuyuan), but at −0.108 when their opponents call them "Donglin Party" (Donglin Dang). One name change, a gap of 0.184—nearly the entire distance between "junzi" (gentleman) and "xiaoren" (petty man). And in the late Qing: Western political terms imported from Japanese between 1898 and 1911 split into two tracks—"yihui" (parliament), "guohui" (national assembly), "xuehui" (learned society) landed smoothly (polarity around +0.05), but "zhengdang" (political party), "mindang" (people's party) remained condemned (−0.077 and −0.103). The "hui" lineage passes, the "dang" lineage is blocked—this is the last manifestation of the precise morphology of the prohibition.

Putting these findings together gives a three-mechanism framework: territorial corporate organization, discursive legitimacy of horizontal association (this paper's focus), and dissolution of consanguineous community. This refines Acemoglu & Robinson's (2019) "narrow corridor" model for the Chinese context. Singapore, currently the only stable "Free Chinese" polity, succeeded by simultaneously reforming all three mechanisms.

1. Introduction

1.1 A Sharp Question

On 24 November 1894, Sun Yat-sen founded the Xingzhonghui (Revive China Association) in Honolulu. "Xingzhonghui" sounds unremarkable today, but it was a carefully chosen name. In late-nineteenth-century Chinese political vocabulary, "hui" was a special character—it carried connotations of sacrifice (the rites of the altars of soil and grain), covenant (oath-bound assemblies), gathering (lecture meetings), and none of the stigma of factional collusion. It was not "dang"; not "meng" (盟, by then heavily negativized in the late Qing); not "she" (社, whose Late Ming failure under the Fushe showed it could be turned against its bearers). In Chinese political discourse, "hui" was almost the only safe island left.

This kind of lexical avoidance was not Sun Yat-sen's personal idiosyncrasy. The Guangxu Emperor's "Mingding Guoshi" edict of 11 June 1898, declaring the Hundred Days' Reform—several hundred characters, not one "zhengdang." Kang Youwei and Liang Qichao's reform organization was called "Baoguohui"—again, "hui." In 1903 Cai Yuanpei and Zhang Taiyan founded the "Guangfuhui." In 1905 Chinese students in Tokyo organized their anti-Qing alliance as the "Tongmenghui"—"tongmeng" was itself a semantic defense, but it still needed "hui" as anchor. When Song Jiaoren reorganized the Tongmenghui into the Kuomintang in 1912, he had to use "guo" (national) to lend legitimacy to "dang"; the experiment collapsed the next year when Song was assassinated.

Behind every lexical choice is the same question: why is "dang" always an attack-word in Chinese while "hui" is legitimate? Why does every reformer trying to organize a supra-kinship, durable political body capable of dialogue with supreme authority have to walk carefully around the character "dang"?

This question is sharper than it first appears. It is not "why did China fall behind"—around 1750, China and Europe were broadly comparable in per capita income, urbanization, commercial complexity (Wong 1997; Pomeranz 2000). Nor is it "why no democracy"—democracy is a rather late political form requiring not only institutions but also specific discursive soil. The question is: given multiple windows in which China possessed material conditions, bureaucratic rationality, and commercial prosperity, why did none yield a legitimate political subject capable of constraining supreme authority and composed of non-kin members?

To answer this, one must enter the internals of Chinese political vocabulary. But tracking a single character ("dang") or a single period (Northern Song factional strife) is insufficient—a character's fate is co-defined by the cluster of characters around it. Why is "dang" an attack-word? The answer cannot be found in "dang" alone; it must be found in the relations among "dang", "hui", "she", "meng", "pai", "tu", "qun", "xue" and other horizontal-collective candidates. This paper therefore scans all such candidates at once, mapping which are permitted, which condemned, which oscillate. The detail of Sun Yat-sen choosing "hui" instead of "dang" is thereby placed back inside twenty-five centuries of semantic-field evolution.

1.2 Three Existing Explanations and Their Limits

Existing research offers three families of answers to "why did China not develop constitutionalism." Each has done substantial work; each has a horizon it cannot cross.

First, material-condition explanations—commerce, technology, military, geography (Pomeranz 2000; Allen 2009; Mokyr 1990). Wong (1997) and Pomeranz (2000) of the "California school" have already closed this path: by 1750 China and Europe were comparable on multiple key indicators. With material conditions similar but political trajectories divergent, the answer obviously lies elsewhere.

Second, political-institutional explanations, focusing on imperial sovereignty structure, bureaucratic organization, and the absence of merchant-military elite coalitions (Greif 2006; Mokyr & Tabellini 2023; Acemoglu & Robinson 2019; Stasavage 2020; Zhang 2018). The latest move is Mokyr & Tabellini's "territorial corporations vs. kinship lineages": European territorial corporations (guilds, chartered cities, parliaments) had property rights and bargaining capacity to face the central state as legal counterparties; Chinese lineages structurally cannot play this role—they supplement the state, they do not constrain it. Powerful argument, but with two limits. First, Ogilvie (2019) has demonstrated that European guilds were predominantly rent-seeking organizations—"can bargain" and "promotes development" are not the same and must be kept distinct. Second, more importantly: this explanation reaches only organizational form, not the discursive conditions. Organizational absence and discursive-legitimacy absence are two sides of the same coin—the former in land and institutions, the latter in vocabulary and semantics. They are mutually causal.

Third, discursive-institutional explanations, whose fullest version is Ari Levine (2005, 2008). His close reading of late Northern Song political texts found: Northern Song political vocabulary itself made legitimate horizontal association among bureaucratic elites impossible at the discursive level. The Qingli reformers tried to redefine factionalism by distinguishing "junzi zhi peng" (gentlemen's friendship) from "xiaoren zhi dang" (petty men's faction), but failed quickly; the subsequent political cycle—Wang Anshi vs. Sima Guang (1069–86), Yuanyou conservatives' backlash (1086–93), Shaosheng reformers' revenge (1093–1100), to Cai Jing's blacklist (1100–04)—none ended in institutional accommodation; all escalated to political purge.

Levine's reading remains the most acute on Chinese political discourse, covering the key 1067–1104 window. But his work leaves three open questions: does the prohibition extend beyond Northern Song? Are there legitimate horizontal-association categories other than "pengdang"? Where do these categories' boundaries with "pengdang" lie? Answering requires crossing a longer time, covering more candidates, with quantitatively verifiable methods—this paper's design.

1.3 A Question Across Six Traditions

The factional prohibition has been identified by six scholarly traditions from different angles, but never tested quantitatively in one unified framework.

(1) Classical exegesis is the oldest. He Yan's Collected Commentaries on the Analects cites Kong Anguo: "Dang: to assist. Junzi though many do not privately assist; they accord with righteousness." Huang Kan, Zhu Xi, Liu Baonan—two millennia of exegetical consensus on the negativity of "dang" has never wavered.

(2) Republican to contemporary Sinology on factional strife. Xiao Gongquan's History of Chinese Political Thought (1945) treats factionalism as a thread of Chinese political thought. Chen Yinke's Discussion of Tang Political History (1944) built a paradigm explaining the Dangguzhihuo (Han partisan prohibition) and the Niu-Li factional strife from two layers: social-bloc (ruling-class lineages) and inner-outer court mirror (outer scholar-officials and inner eunuchs). Kawakatsu Yoshio's Studies on the Six-Dynasties Aristocratic Society (1982) explains the Dangguzhihuo from a "pure stream"–village opinion network angle, with a striking reverse narrative—the "pure stream" suppressed by the prohibition became the seed of the aristocratic system at the Wei-Jin transition. Utsunomiya Kiyoyoshi (1955) explains via centrifugal magnate economics. Yu Yingshi's Zhu Xi's Historical World (2003) systematically argues the Northern Song scholar-officials' "co-governance" subjectivity and the "constitutionalization of state policy" mechanism.

(3) Contemporary Sinology's discursive-political analysis. Peter Bol's "This Culture of Ours" (1992) traces the Tang-Song transformation of the "shi" concept from hereditary aristocracy to classicist bureaucracy. Hilde De Weerdt's Information, Territory, and Networks (2015) systematically documents the trans-regional discursive network Southern Song literati built through notebooks, court gazettes, maps, and collected works. Deng Xiaonan's The Law of the Ancestors (2006) reveals the Northern Song "ancestral law" as a discursive norm—a stillborn thin constitutionalism—and the process by which both factions appealed to it and mutually dissolved its force.

(4) Late Ming Donglin studies. Hucker (1957) was the first to clearly distinguish the Donglin movement (self-identification: scholarly-moral movement) from the Donglin faction (enemy naming: political faction). Ono Kazuko's Studies on Late Ming Factions and Societies (1962) pushed this distinction to its limit—she documented that core members like Gu Xiancheng and Gao Panlong never called themselves "Donglin Party"; the label "Donglin Party" first emerged at scale in the 1611 Li Sancai case. Dardess's Blood and History in China (2002) records the inflation of the "Donglin Party" label during the Tianqi-era persecution: from a core of dozens to a blacklist of 309, the vast majority of whom had never lectured at the Donglin Academy.

(5) Comparative institutional analysis. Greif (1994, 2006) on medieval Genoa reveals "path B"—through the self-enforcing design of the podestà institution, 145 years of commercial-revolution stability achieved without territorial corporations, finally eroding because the underlying lineage structure was not dissolved. Rubin (2017) on Islamic societies reveals a phenomenon mechanism-level near-isomorphic to China's factional prohibition—a self-reinforcing equilibrium of legitimacy exchange between sultan and ulama, excluding merchant elites from the bargaining table. Stasavage (2020) proposes a global comparative framework for "early democracy."

(6) The methodology of computational conceptual history. Hamilton, Leskovec & Jurafsky (2016) laid the foundation for diachronic word-embedding comparison; Garg, Schiebinger, Jurafsky & Zou (2018, PNAS) quantified a century of US gender and ethnic stereotypes; Kozlowski, Taddy & Evans (2019, ASR) used antonym-pair mean projection to analyze class culture; Rodman (2020, Political Analysis) tracked 161 years of "equality." This toolkit is now mature; this paper is its first large-scale application to twenty-five centuries of Chinese political vocabulary.

Each tradition sees one facet: exegesis sees character-level negativization; Chen Yinke and Kawakatsu see socio-political mechanisms; Bol and Yu see the shi's subjectivity transformation; De Weerdt sees the existence-but-insufficiency of information flow; Levine sees Northern Song discursive rhetoric; Hucker, Ono, Dardess see the self/other asymmetry of Late Ming naming; Rubin sees cross-civilizational legitimacy lock-in. This paper places these facets into one analytical framework—through full-spectrum scanning of Chinese political vocabulary, integrating six traditions' qualitative observations into a quantifiably verifiable semantic-field evolutionary narrative.

1.4 Three Findings and a New Claim

Using the word-embedding method of Mikolov et al. (2013), this paper trains ten independent period-specific models on a 104-million-character classical Chinese corpus spanning the pre-Qin to late Qing, covering pre-Qin, Western Han, Eastern Han, Tang, Song, Yuan, early-mid Ming, late Ming, early-mid Qing, late Qing. About 130 candidate words—organized in seven semantic layers (friendship, moral identity, ethical principles, scholarly activity, oath, collective decision, community, corporate candidates)—are full-spectrum scanned, with each word's polarity calculated per period.

Finding 1: Chinese political vocabulary does not lack "collective vocabulary"; it precisely lacks one specific category. Scanning shows that L1–L6 and L7a (community) candidates are consistently legitimate or oscillating across ten dynasties—friendship (you +0.151, pengyou +0.066), morality (junzi, xian +0.222), ethics (ren +0.262, gongyi +0.327), scholarship (xue +0.083, jiangxue +0.066, shuyuan +0.055), oath (meng +0.037, jieyi +0.106), collective decision (gongyi Han peak +0.227), community (zongzu +0.034, jishe +0.052).

Only L7b—the "individual-absorbed-into-undifferentiated-collective" candidates—are uniformly negative: dang −0.095, chai −0.079, tu −0.077, lü −0.077, bei −0.074, lei −0.070, qun −0.060, zhong −0.041, wu −0.025, pai −0.023, tuan −0.011. The compounds in this layer are more deeply prohibited: pengdang −0.130, qundang −0.097, dangzheng −0.108, sidang −0.204, yandang −0.130. The sole exception is "hui" (μ = +0.049)—it escaped the prohibition and consequently bears nearly all legitimate horizontal-association functions, structural reasons analyzed in §5.

Finding 2: The prohibited category corresponds to universitas in the Berman-Tierney triad.

Chinese discourse did not develop a category corresponding to universitas. The real form of the factional prohibition lies here: it is not generic suppression of all "collectivity," but the prohibition of one specific kind of collectivity—the kind that acts as a legitimate political subject, composed of undifferentiated members, capable of absorbing individuals and speaking on their behalf.

Finding 3: The prohibition targets a semantic structure ("acting as a legitimate political subject"), not specific characters. The same root in different compounds can fall into completely different semantic fields. "Hui" as single character is legitimate (+0.049), but with concrete political content compounds branch:

  • Legitimate: yihui (+0.042), guohui (+0.057), xuehui (+0.072), jianghui (+0.064), Baoguohui (+0.073)
  • Contrast (without "hui"): pengdang (−0.130)

  • Legitimate: jishe (+0.052), sheji (+0.043), gongshe (implicit +0.178)
  • Stigmatized: Fushe (Late Ming −0.137), Jishe (−0.102), jieshe (Late Qing −0.117)

"Pai" as single character is near-neutral (−0.023), but xuepai in the late Qing reaches −0.109.

The factional prohibition does not operate at the character layer—single characters "hui," "she," "meng" are legitimate—but at the semantic-structure layer: any non-kin collective identified as "acting as a legitimate political subject," regardless of what character it bears, gets pulled into prohibited territory. This is more refined than "Chinese suppresses certain characters."

New claim: China did not develop constitutionalism not because Chinese discourse lacks "collective vocabulary"—Chinese has complete conjuratio (meng, shi, qi, yue), communitas (she, xiangdang, zongzu), collective deliberation (gongyi, gonglun, tingyi), and scholarly combination (jianghui, shuyuan) legitimate categories. What China precisely lacks is the universitas category—"undifferentiated members as legitimate collective political subject." From the pre-Qin Analects' "qun er bu dang" onward, across the judicial institutionalization of the Han partisan prohibition, the Northern Song Qingli discursive war, the Late Ming Donglin naming war, the late-Qing introduction of "zhengdang"—twenty-five centuries of stable operation.

Combined into a three-mechanism framework—territorial corporate organization, discursive legitimacy of horizontal association (this paper's focus), dissolution of consanguineous community—this refines the Acemoglu & Robinson (2019) "narrow corridor" model for the Chinese context, and for the first time provides systematic quantitative evidence for "discursive legitimacy prohibition" as an institutional form parallel to "institutional path dependence."

2. Theoretical Framework

2.1 Levine's Hypothesis and its Reception

Ari Levine's Divided by a Common Language (2008, building on Levine 2005) advances three nested claims about Northern Song political discourse. First, the core function of "pengdang" in Northern Song political practice is not description but attack—every political group uses "pengdang" to accuse opponents while firmly denying its own factional character. This "use against others, deny for oneself" asymmetry prevents "pengdang" from evolving into a neutral descriptive category. Second, Fan Zhongyan and Ouyang Xiu's 1043–44 attempt to open discursive space for legitimate horizontal association by distinguishing "gentlemen's friendship" from "petty men's faction" did not succeed. Third, imperial authority is the ultimate arbiter—any nascent horizontal association can have its legitimacy revoked at any time.

Levine's work has been widely accepted (Google Scholar citations exceed 380 as of 2026); his close-reading precision remains unsurpassed. This paper extends Levine in three directions: the analytical period from Northern Song's thirty-seven years to twenty-five centuries spanning pre-Qin to late Qing; qualitative rhetorical analysis converted to quantitatively verifiable word-embedding observation; "pengdang" as a single word placed back into its entire semantic field, identifying the precise boundary of the prohibition (which roots are prohibited, which are not, where the boundary lies). Levine clarified Northern Song "pengdang" as an asymmetrically used attack tool; this paper further clarifies the precise categorical boundary of this mechanism's operation, and its stable form across longer time.

2.2 A Key Conceptual Resource: The Berman-Tierney Triad

To see clearly what China lacks, we borrow a tool from European legal history. This tool is rarely cited in Chinese studies but is crucial for the argument here. Harold Berman's Law and Revolution: The Formation of the Western Legal Tradition (1983) systematically argues that the 12th–13th century European "Papal Revolution," and the legal science that followed, were built on three independent but mutually supporting legitimating terms—universitas, communitas, conjuratio. Brian Tierney's Foundations of the Conciliar Theory (1955) traced even earlier the development of universitas in the hands of medieval canon lawyers. Together these two books establish a crucial point: what Europe developed is not "one kind" of collective vocabulary but three mutually specialized categories of collectivity.

Universitas (legal person) is the most crucial. Its Latin literal meaning is "wholeness," but in medieval law it acquired a precise technical sense: a collective composed of undifferentiated members, acting as a single legal subject. Its core feature is individual absorption—once an individual joins a universitas, when acting as member he no longer acts in his own name but in the name of the whole. The corporation can therefore own property, sign contracts, sue, be sued—it is a fabricated legal person. Medieval European universities (universitas magistrorum et scholarium), cities (universitas civium), guilds (universitas mercatorum), monastic orders (universitas religiosorum), and later territorial corporations all belong to universitas. Mokyr-Tabellini's (2023) "territorial corporate organization" is just universitas concretized at the geographic-political level.

Communitas (community) is another kind, pointing to territorial, ritual, internally graded collectives. Medieval European villages, parishes, provinces, nations (natio) are all communitas. The key difference from universitas: communitas does not require absorption—members retain their original differences, status, family belonging. It is closer to "who we are" than to "we as single subject acting."

Conjuratio (sworn association) is the third, pointing to contractual, oath-bound, dissoluble collectives. Most medieval European cities began as conjuratio: a group of citizens swearing to each other, signing contracts, paying joint deposits, forming a union of mutual protection and mutual constraint. The crucial feature is contractuality—members join through explicit agreement and can in principle leave by dissolving the agreement. Crusades, commercial leagues, knightly orders, citizens' uprisings all belong to this category.

In medieval Europe these three are layered, not mutually exclusive. A specific city could simultaneously be universitas civium (legal person), communitas (territorial community), and conjuratio (citizens' sworn association). But functionally each does different work: universitas provides legal personhood and bargaining capacity, communitas provides territorial belonging and cultural identity, conjuratio provides organizational origin and mutual contractual constraint. Only when all three coexist can a sustainable political subject capable of constraining supreme authority, composed of non-kin members, form.

§4's full-spectrum scan will show: Chinese discourse developed legitimate conjuratio and communitas categories—meng, shi, qi, yue, jieyi, huimeng serve conjuratio; she, xiangdang, zongzu, jishe serve communitas—but as "legitimate collective with members absorbed," universitas's candidate roots (dang, qun, tu, bei, lei, chai) are systematically stigmatized.

"Why China did not develop constitutionalism" is hereby refined: not a generic "collective vocabulary absence," but a precise specific-category absence, and this category can be precisely pointed out.

2.4 Three Mechanisms—Chinese-context refinement of the A-R framework

Acemoglu & Robinson's The Narrow Corridor (2019) proposes a two-factor model: entering the "narrow corridor" (state-society balance) requires state and society both strong, mutually constrained via "Red Queen effects." China is placed in the "despotic Leviathan" category. The A-R framework's handling of China has been criticized as too coarse (Borràs Arumí et al. question its "straw-man" China account). More important than critiquing A-R is to see: A-R treats "society" as a single pole, which is insufficient for China; it must be unpacked. This paper splits the "society" pole into three independent mechanisms.

Mechanism 1: Territorial corporate organization (universitas). Mokyr-Tabellini (2023)'s core variable. European territorial corporations—guilds, chartered cities, parliaments, Estates-General—had property rights and bargaining capacity to face the central state as legal counterparties. Chinese lineages structurally cannot play this role. Ogilvie (2019) poses a fundamental challenge to M-T: European guilds were primarily rent-seeking. But Ogilvie's critique is normative (rent-seeking is bad), not structural (territorial corporations don't exist)—the "guild-elite rent-seeking coalition" she describes is a bargaining structure, aligned in direction with M-T's core claim. Mechanism 1 is therefore precisely stated as "existence of territorial corporations as sustainable bargaining counterparties."

Mechanism 2: Discursive legitimacy of horizontal association (existence of the universitas category). The core variable this paper aims to quantitatively isolate from the other two. Precise statement: in Chinese discourse, all candidate roots corresponding to universitas (dang, qun, tu, bei, lei, chai) have been systematically negativized for twenty-five centuries from pre-Qin to late Qing. §4 provides systematic quantitative evidence.

Mechanism 3: Dissolution of consanguineous community. The variable Ōtsuka (1966) identified and long ignored by Chinese scholarship. Asian community (kyōdōtai) is a kinship-based production unit organizing land, labor, and exchange by reciprocity rather than market logic. Capitalism could sprout in medieval European cities because the Church continually banned cousin marriage from the 4th–7th centuries onward, actively breaking the consanguineous community; Asian societies never went through this rupture.

Relations among the three mechanisms. The three are analytically independent—each can in principle exist or fail alone—but empirically mutually reinforcing. Tokugawa Japan had Mechanism 1 (260+ daimyō domains) but lacked Mechanisms 2 and 3, resulting in Meiji authoritarian modernization. Joseon Korea had Mechanism 1 (1,000+ academies) but Mechanisms 2 and 3 failed, no constitutional result. Medieval Genoa lacked Mechanism 1 but achieved 145 years of commercial-revolution stability through podestà self-enforcement; the podesteria ultimately self-eroded because underlying lineage was not dissolved but reinforced. Only when all three are present does stable constitutional development emerge—the argument of §6's cross-civilizational comparison.

2.5 Defining Constitutionalism Precisely

One final theoretical clarification. This paper adopts the thin, constraining definition from Stasavage (2020) and Acemoglu & Robinson (2019), decomposed into three progressive levels.

Level A (minimum threshold): institutionalized, cross-cutting non-state entities exist as legitimate counterparties to supreme-authority decisions—i.e., universitas in the Mokyr-Tabellini sense.

Level B (early democracy, Stasavage sense): these counterparties hold active consent rights on specific decisions, not merely being consulted. This requires that horizontal associations be not only physically present but also discursively acknowledged as legitimate political subjects.

Level C (modern democracy): Level B consent rights are normalized into representative institutions, with periodic elections and non-imperative mandates.

China failed at Level A—this is the paper's core positioning. What needs explaining is not "why China did not develop modern representation" (which presupposes Level B in place), but "why China did not develop minimum-threshold cross-cutting legitimate political subject." This positioning also clarifies the inaccuracy of Stasavage's claim that "as far back as we have historical writing in China, we don't see traditions or histories of council governance or early democracy." §4.4 will show: Han-era "gongyi" in word-embedding space has strong positive polarity (Western Han +0.165, Eastern Han +0.232—exceeding the Northern Song peak +0.181). Han China did have a Level-B legitimate discursive space—suppressed in Tang, reactivated in Song—but never rose to Level A. L6 (gongyi) legitimacy oscillated across dynasties; universitas as legitimate political subject was always absent.

3. Data and Method

3.1 Corpus Construction

This paper's empirical foundation is daizhigev20 (Daizhige Ancient Texts Corpus v2.0), currently the largest open-source classical Chinese corpus (6.9 GB, 15,694 text files). On this base, ten period-specific corpora are constructed corresponding to major Chinese historical periods, totaling 103.8 million characters: pre-Qin (1.9 M), Western Han (2.5 M), Eastern Han (2.6 M), Tang (6.8 M), Song (22.0 M), Yuan (3.5 M), early-mid Ming (15.1 M), late Ming (6.5 M), early-mid Qing (19.6 M), late Qing (23.3 M). The corpus covers elite political discourse—dynastic histories, collected works, memorials, veritable records, Confucian classics—not folk vernacular literature or religious texts. This boundary defines the scope of the paper's claims.

3.2 Polarity Score

Polarity follows the standard method in computational conceptual history (Hamilton et al. 2016; Garg et al. 2018; Kozlowski et al. 2019), defined for word w as the difference between mean cosine to positive anchors and mean cosine to negative anchors. Primary anchor sets: $A_+ = \{$jun, zhong, xian, ren, yi, gong$\}$; $A_- = \{$xiao, jian, ning, xie, si$\}$. The formula is functionally equivalent to Garg et al. (2018)'s relative norm difference. Four independent anchor families (moral, legal, court-private, loyalty) are also computed in parallel; results agree qualitatively.

3.3 Full-Spectrum Scan Design

Per §2.3's seven-layer structure, all candidate words capable of bearing "horizontal legitimate association" function are listed for scanning—including both core words from existing literature (dang, pengdang, gongyi, shuyuan) and less-studied roots (chai, tu, bei, wu, lü, she, hui, meng). Total: about 130 candidates × 10 dynasties = approximately 1,300 polarity observations. For each candidate, polarity is computed per dynasty (using its vector if in vocabulary, or implicit compound vector of constituent characters otherwise). The output is a 130 × 10 polarity matrix, the empirical basis of §4.

3.4 Cross-Period Alignment and Bootstrap CI

Polarity comparison uses within-space relative quantities (Kozlowski 2019 demonstrates such projections need no Procrustes alignment). Online appendix A.5 provides a Procrustes-aligned robustness check; results agree. Pre-Qin corpus at 1.9 M characters falls into the "small corpus" category; per Antoniak & Mimno (2018), bootstrap CI is reported for key analyses.

3.5 Polysemy Disambiguation

"Dang" has at least three independent senses: administrative unit ("five hundred families form a dang"), kinship lineage ("father's dang, mother's dang"), factional clique ("qun er bu dang", "e dang"). Context-rule-based disambiguation: for each occurrence of "dang," scan ±15 characters and classify as factional, kinship, administrative, or unspecified; retokenize and retrain word2vec. §4.3 reports: the factional sense yields sim(dang, si) = +0.463; administrative and kinship senses are near 0—the prohibition precisely targets the factional sense.

4. Empirical Findings

This chapter proceeds from broad to narrow: full-spectrum landscape of the semantic field (§4.1–§4.2), internal structure of categories (§4.3–§4.5), root vs. compound divergence (§4.6), operation of the prohibition in specific historical events (§4.7–§4.9), and finally the Donglin naming experiment and the late-Qing dual track (§4.10–§4.11). Starting from the whole semantic field, "dang"'s fate is placed back in its larger genealogy. (For interactive figures, switch to the Chinese version.)

4.1 Anchor Validation

Before presenting main results, we confirm the polarity tool itself correctly distinguishes canonical positive/negative reference points across ten corpora. Across all ten periods, "junzi" and "zhong" consistently carry positive polarity; "jian" is strongly negative; "xiaoren" is negative or near-zero in most periods. The polarity tool correctly distinguishes positive/negative references across the 2,500-year span. Internal cohesion checks (online appendix A.4) show stable semantic axes for both anchor sets in all ten periods.

4.2 Full-Spectrum Scan: The Landscape of Legitimate Categories

Before analyzing any specific character (like "dang"), we present the landscape. A clear three-way structure emerges:

  • Group 1 (legitimate categories): L1 friendship, L2 moral identity, L3 ethical principles, L4 scholarly activity—consistently legitimate across ten dynasties (ten-dynasty mean significantly positive).
  • Group 2 (mildly positive, oscillating): L5 Conjuratio, L6 collective decision, L7a Communitas, L7b "legitimate-compound" candidates—broadly legitimate or oscillating, with ten-dynasty mean still positive.
  • Group 3 (consistently prohibited): L7b single characters (−0.049), L7b prohibited compounds (−0.108)—consistently prohibited across ten dynasties, intensity close to the "negative control" group (−0.169).

Chinese discourse is not generically prohibiting "collective vocabulary"—it precisely prohibits one specific category while leaving six others free to develop.

4.3 Three Stable Legitimate Categories

L1–L3 stable legitimacy. you (μ = +0.151), xian (+0.222), ren (+0.262), yi (+0.222), zhong (+0.233), gongyi (+0.327, among the highest). These words form the basic semantic field of "individual morality," with strong positive polarity from pre-Qin to late Qing, low standard deviation (mostly 0.03–0.06), cross-dynasty stable. They bear the legitimate function of "individual as moral subject"—but none functions as "collective as political subject." For the latter we must look at L4 and above.

L4 scholarly legitimacy. xue (+0.083), jiangxue (+0.066), jianghui (+0.064), daoxue (+0.027), lixue (+0.072), shuyuan (+0.055)—all legitimate. This is the strongest legitimate form Chinese discourse allows for "non-kin members organizing for joint action"—but with a precondition: the organization must be identified as scholarly, not political. §4.6's boundary probe will show this precondition's fragility: once "X xuepai" carries political meaning (late-Qing "xuepai" −0.109), it gets pulled into prohibited territory.

L5 Conjuratio mildly positive. meng (+0.037), shi (+0.034), qi (+0.010), yue (+0.014), jieyi (+0.106), huimeng (+0.058)—all mildly positive, bearing the legitimate function of contractual, oath-bound, dissoluble horizontal association. Jieyi (μ = +0.106) is particularly high—the cleanest Chinese form of "non-kin individuals mutually contracting." The "Peach Garden oath" of Guan Yu, Liu Bei, Zhang Fei is the most representative form of Chinese conjuratio. The legitimacy declined after late Ming: meng from Song +0.151 to late Qing −0.109; jiemeng from pre-Qin +0.067 to late Qing −0.098; tongmeng from Song +0.177 to late Qing −0.060. Even at this trough, however, it remains significantly higher than universitas candidate roots.

L7a Communitas mildly positive. she (+0.001), zongzu (+0.034), zhouli (+0.053), jishe (+0.052), sheji (+0.043). These are the Chinese forms of "territorial + kinship + ritual" legitimate collectives, bearing communitas's function—providing "who we are" belonging without requiring absorption into a single political subject.

L1–L3, L5, L7a together constitute the full inventory of Chinese discourse's legitimate collective vocabulary. This inventory is far richer than commonly thought—covering five layers: friendship, moral identity, ethical principles, scholarly activity, oath association, community. Chinese does not lack legitimate forms of "non-kin members organizing." So why did China still not develop constitutionalism? The answer is not in these five layers—they are all legitimate. The answer is in the remaining two: L6 (collective decision) and L7b (universitas).

4.4 An Oscillating Legitimate Category: L6 Gongyi (directly rebutting Stasavage)

L6 (collective decision) has overall mean +0.032—mildly positive. But this mean masks an important temporal structure. Looking at "gongyi" specifically: pre-Qin +0.109, Western Han +0.165, Eastern Han +0.232, Tang −0.138, Song +0.181, Yuan −0.074, early-mid Ming +0.077, late Ming +0.113, early-mid Qing +0.063, late Qing +0.034.

First, Han is the peak. Eastern Han +0.232—highest of the ten periods, exceeding the Northern Song peak +0.181. Han-era "gongyi" sat in a stable positive semantic field, directly related to imperial-court collective consultation, exemplified by court deliberations (tingyi, chaoyi, boshiyi) extensively recorded in the Hanshu and Hou Hanshu.

This directly rebuts Stasavage (2020). Stasavage explicitly states: "as far back as we have historical writing in China, we don't see traditions or histories of council governance or early democracy." This paper's evidence shows this is inaccurate. Han "gongyi" has strong positive polarity in word-embedding space, with semantic neighbors pointing to specific collective deliberation mechanisms (tingyi, chaoyi, boshiyi, yizheng). Han China did have a Stasavage-sense Level-B legitimate discursive space—only it never rose to Level A (legitimate political subject), because L7b (universitas) was always prohibited.

Second, the Tang trough is striking. Tang polarity −0.138—the only negative value in the entire series. This reflects the special structure of Tang politics: an aristocratic-military polity displaces Confucian-bureaucratic collective deliberation from the center. Peter Bol's (1992) "This Culture of Ours" provides the mechanism: Tang "shi" are hereditary aristocrats; only Song "shi" transformed into examination-recruited bureaucrats; "gongyi" as collective-deliberation vocabulary depends on the shi's political subjectivity.

Third, Song is a revival of the Han precedent. Northern Song polarity recovers to +0.181—re-legitimization, not first legitimization. Yu Yingshi's (2003) "constitutionalization of state policy"—the institutionalization of Northern Song "guoshi" as the legitimacy foundation co-set by emperor and scholar-officials—can be reread as a restoration of Han collective-deliberation vocabulary.

This temporal picture complicates the simple "factional prohibition" narrative and makes it more precise: L6 (collective decision) was never permanently locked—it cycled through legitimation and suppression following dynasties' political-philosophical orientations; L7b (universitas)—the real core target of the prohibition—shows uniformly negative polarity across all ten periods. This "L6 mutable, L7b invariant" asymmetric structure is the most precise morphological description of the factional prohibition.

4.5 A Consistently Prohibited Category: L7b Universitas

L7b is the core category of this paper's claim and the most consistently, precisely prohibited. Single characters ranked by prohibition intensity (ten-dynasty mean): dang −0.095, chai −0.079, tu −0.077, lü −0.077, bei −0.074, lei −0.070, qun −0.060, zhong −0.041, wu −0.025, pai −0.023, tuan −0.011, hui +0.049.

An obvious clustering structure: dang, chai, tu, lü, bei, lei, qun consistently negative (μ between −0.06 and −0.10), cross-dynasty stable, standard deviation 0.04–0.06. Zhong, wu, pai, tuan in a middle transition zone. "Hui" is the only escape.

Why this clustering? The semantic core. The prohibited group shares a specific core—individual absorbed into undifferentiated collective:

  • dang: secretly forming cliques (peng bi wei jian, "qun er bu dang")
  • qun: indistinguishable from the crowd ("qun ju zhong ri yan bu ji yi")
  • tu: swept into a trend (yi shi zhi tu, tudang)
  • bei: assimilated into a generation (wu bei zhi chi)
  • lei: classified as one kind (bu lei junzi)
  • chai: listed in the same rank (chai bei xiang jin)
  • lü: drawn into accompaniment (tong lü, non-individual)

The common feature: canceling individual difference, absorbing members into an undifferentiated collective identity. This precisely corresponds to universitas's core: "members absorbed as undifferentiated into legitimate collective." Chinese discourse precisely prohibits this semantic structure.

The escaping "hui." Why does "hui" escape? Its semantic core is fundamentally different: contractuality, visibility, members preserved—a meeting (a concrete, bounded event), a covenant (an explicit contract), a gathering (a temporary, dispersible assembly). Members are not absorbed—they participate as individuals and return to themselves when the meeting ends. This is the conjuratio core, not the universitas core. Because of this distinction, "hui" came to bear nearly all functions of "non-kin members legitimately organizing" in Chinese discourse: Xingzhonghui, Baoguohui, Guangfuhui, Tongmenghui, xuehui, yihui, guohui.

Compounds are more deeply prohibited. L7b prohibited compounds have ten-dynasty mean −0.108, deeper than single characters (−0.049). The deepening points to a specific mechanism: not "dang" as character, but any compound identified as "specific political collective": pengdang −0.130, qundang −0.097, sidang −0.204 (one of the most negative), yandang −0.130, qundao −0.147, qunxiong −0.128, bidang −0.096, edang −0.069. "Sidang" (private faction, μ = −0.204) is the most negative among all universitas compounds—matching the §4.3 finding that the factional sense of "dang" yields sim(dang, si) = +0.463: "si" and "dang" have fused into an inseparable core structure in Chinese discourse.

4.6 Boundary Probe: Single Character vs. Compound Divergence

Single "hui" is legitimate but Late Ming Fushe and Jishe are stigmatized. Single "she" is neutral but "jieshe" sinks in the late Qing. "Pai" as single is near-neutral (−0.023), but "xuepai" in the late Qing is −0.109. The "single legitimate / compound polluted" phenomenon reveals the prohibition's precise mechanism, and deserves a dedicated look.

Table 5 (in the Chinese version) reveals the mechanism: a single root's legitimacy can be broken by a compound. A root may itself be legitimate, but once compounded with "specific political content" or "identifiable political collective," it can be pulled into prohibited territory.

Mechanism 1: Compounds carry more specific "political subject" content. "Hui" as single character refers to an abstract gathering event; "Fushe" and "Jishe" specifically refer to particular Late Ming political-scholarly collectives. Once readers identify such a collective as "acting as political subject," word-embedding space pulls it adjacent to "X dang."

Mechanism 2: Compounds are reverse-polluted by their usage contexts. "Fushe" in Late Ming corpus has nearest neighbors di, shan, zun, ji, li—all negative actions. This means "Fushe" was used primarily in contexts of being attacked. Word embeddings learn distributional similarity, which reflects usage context, so "Fushe" is automatically learned as neighbor to negative actions.

The prohibition operates at the semantic-structure layer ("acting as legitimate political subject"), not the character layer. Reforming the prohibition cannot be achieved by creating a new character (e.g., importing "zhengdang" from Japanese in 1898)—once the new character is identified as carrying the universitas semantic structure, it is automatically pulled into prohibited territory. §4.11 will show the specific mechanism of "zhengdang" remaining prohibited despite being a new word in the late Qing.

4.7 The Dangguzhihuo (Han Partisan Prohibition): Judicial Institutionalization

§4.5 has shown that "dang" is uniformly negative across dynasties. But this negativity already existed—originating in the pre-Qin Analects' "qun er bu dang." Then what role does the Dangguzhihuo (166–184) play in this long-period narrative?

Quantitative evidence. "Dangu" as compound: 0 occurrences in Western Han corpus, 39 in Eastern Han corpus—a clear semantic-coinage event. A new compound was created and repeatedly used over the 166–184 two-plus decades, entering the core inventory of Chinese political vocabulary. More crucially, "dangren" in Eastern Han corpus has semantic neighbors: su (0.42), song (0.38), yuan (0.36), wu (0.34), he (0.32), yan (0.30)—all pointing to specific actions of judicial persecution. This constitutes a complete judicial-persecution semantic field: accused (su, song), framed (wu), impeached (he), convicted (yan), claiming wrongful conviction (yuan).

The Dangguzhihuo is hereby repositioned: not the origin of negativization (negativity predates it by seven centuries), but the joint of judicial institutionalization—encoding a long-existing semantic load into formal legal-administrative practice, thereby extending 1,700 years.

Specific persecution records. Quantitative evidence alone is thin; it must be coupled with historical narrative. The First Prohibition (166, Emperor Huan): the eunuchs used the sorcerer Zhang Cheng's disciple Lao Xiu to file an accusation against Li Ying and others—"raising imperial-university wandering scholars, allying with disciples from various commanderies, driving each other on, jointly forming a faction (gong wei bu dang), slandering the court, suspected of disordering customs." The accusation's key is "gong wei bu dang"—"bu dang" as a newly coined compound reframes the scholars' teacher-student-friend relations as organized political association. Emperor Huan bypassed the Three Excellencies and sent Li Ying and 200+ others directly to the eunuch-controlled Beisi Prison—a critical procedural inversion: judicial procedure originally belonging to the outer court was transferred to the inner-palace eunuch system. Chen Fan refused to sign, with "those investigated are all renowned across the realm, loyal upright officials caring for the state; even if not pardoned, what crime have they?"—a key evidence of judicial procedure being eunuchized and bypassed.

The Second Prohibition (169): the "gou dang" mechanism reached its limit—conviction of one person extended to his disciples, former subordinates, fathers, sons, brothers, finally five generations. This is among the earliest documented "association crime" cases in Chinese legal history—the core mechanism is punishing not specific criminal acts but the identity of "being a member of a certain collective."

Dialogue with existing traditions. This paper's claim does not replace Chen Yinke's (1944) "ruling-class bloc competition," Kawakatsu's (1982) "pure-stream / village-opinion network," Utsunomiya's (1955) "magnate-imperial economic centrifugation," Yu Yingshi's (1987) "shi's collective consciousness," or Qu Tongzu's (1947) "li-fa unification"—it makes a new explanation at a different layer (vocabulary-computational). Chen and others explain why the Dangguzhihuo occurred; this paper explains what the Dangguzhihuo cast. Complementary, not substitutive.

4.8 Same-Root Different-Suffix Experiment: The Suffix Carries the Load

Strategy B directly isolates the suffix's contribution to polarity. If the same root (e.g., "peng") yields one polarity with "xue" and a completely different polarity with "dang," the polarity difference is purely attributable to the suffix itself.

In Song-period data: + xue (scholarly) yields consistently positive polarity (mean +0.069); + dang (political body) yields consistently strongly negative polarity (mean −0.160). Polarity inversion Δ = 0.229, across six independent roots. This is the most direct evidence that the negativization of L7b universitas is carried by the suffix itself. Wilcoxon paired-rank test gives p < 0.001, rejecting equivalent polarity. Large effect size (0.20+), six independent roots, single direction—not a single-outlier artifact but a structural fact at the semantic-field level.

4.9 Vector Arithmetic: Contagion of the Prohibition

Strategy C: add the "dang" character vector directly onto positive compounds and see its contagion power. If "dang" indeed occupies a negative position in word-embedding space, this addition should produce stable, predictable polarity downshift regardless of starting compound.

Tested in Song corpus: gongyi (+0.106 → −0.032, shift −0.138), gonglun (+0.077 → −0.060, shift −0.137), tongzhi (+0.114 → −0.035, shift −0.149), tongdao (+0.132 → −0.022, shift −0.154), tongxin (+0.033 → −0.092, shift −0.125), gongxin (+0.105 → −0.053, shift −0.158), daoxue (+0.152 → +0.030, shift −0.122), xiangxian (+0.165 → +0.025, shift −0.140). Mean shift −0.140, standard deviation only 0.014. Eight independent starting compounds, all producing about −0.14 stable polarity shift after adding the "dang" vector. The negativity of "dang" is not a byproduct of any specific historical use—it is a property carried by the character's geometric position in word-embedding space itself. Wherever placed—gongyi, daoxue, xiangxian—it pulls the entire compound in the same direction with the same magnitude.

4.10 The Donglin Naming Experiment: Legitimate vs. Prohibited on the Same Group

Strategy D exploits the special historical structure of the Late Ming Donglin movement—the same group of people referred to under three different names in the same period—to construct a near-impossible controlled experiment in history.

Late Ming corpus labels: Donglin Shuyuan (self-naming, communitas + scholarly frame), n=19, polarity +0.054; Donglin (neutral label), n=531, polarity −0.091; Donglin Dang (enemy naming, universitas), n=19, polarity −0.108; pengdang (generic universitas), n=53, polarity −0.137; yandang (specific universitas), n=20, polarity −0.148; junzi (positive control), n=1,025, polarity +0.131.

The only variable is naming. Polarity difference 0.184—nearly the full distance between "junzi" (+0.131) and "xiaoren" (−0.115) in the Late Ming corpus.

The change in semantic neighbors is even sharper. When Donglin is self-named "Shuyuan," nearest neighbors are "shuyuan" and "Pan (Panlong)"—a scholarly neighborhood. When called "Donglin Dang" by enemies, nearest neighbors become "dang (eunuch hat)," "dangren," "dang," "yan (eunuch)." The Late Ming corpus places "Donglin Dang" closer to "yandang" than to "junzi": sim(Donglin Dang, yandang) = +0.240, sim(Donglin Dang, junzi) = +0.038.

Dialogue with existing traditions. This finding precisely confirms three traditions: Hucker (1957)'s movement / faction distinction; Ono Kazuko (1962)'s "self/other asymmetric naming"—core members like Gu Xiancheng and Gao Panlong never called themselves "Donglin Dang"; the label "Donglin Dang" first appeared at scale in the 1611 Li Sancai case; Dardess (2002)'s record of label inflation (from a core of dozens to a blacklist of 309). This paper is the first to elevate this 60-year, three-tradition (Chinese / Japanese / English) qualitative claim into a statistically observable fact.

Micro-textual evidence: Ruan Dacheng and Wang Shaohui's Donglin Roster (1625). This text was presented to Wei Zhongxian as a list for dismissal and persecution—mapping 109 officials identified as "Donglin Dang" one-by-one onto the 108 heroes of the Water Margin (Li Sancai = Timely Rain Song Jiang; Ye Xianggao = Jade Qilin Lu Junyi; Zhao Nanxing = Resourceful Wu Yong; Gao Panlong = Heavenly Star Sun Li; Yang Lian = Heavenly Mighty Zhu Tong, etc.). Every official, censor, and lecturer who used the Roster produced new "Donglin Dang + thief/bandit/traitor/villain" co-occurrences in the corpus—this is the micro-textual production mechanism behind sim(Donglin Dang, yandang) = +0.240.

The Fushe extension. After Donglin, Zhang Pu founded the Fushe in 1629, deliberately avoiding "dang" by self-naming "she." Yet the Fushe still did not escape the prohibition's contagion—opponents named it "Donglin yuyu" (remnants), "Fushe Dang"; by the Hongguang period (1644–45) when Ruan Dacheng compiled the Huangnan Lu, the Fushe had been fully absorbed into the "X Dang" semantic field. The Fushe's polarity of −0.137 in Late Ming corpus is the quantitative trace of this process. The Fushe case corresponds to §4.6's boundary probe: the factional prohibition operates at the semantic-structure layer, regardless of character. Even self-defending with "she," once identified as universitas, it is pulled back into prohibited territory.

4.11 The Late-Qing Dual Track: Hui-Lineage Legitimate / Dang-Lineage Blocked

§4.10 shows the Late Ming naming mechanism. The cleanest modern verification comes from the late Qing—from 1898 to 1911, Western political vocabulary borrowed from Japanese split into two divergent tracks.

Late Qing polarity: yihui (parliament, "hui" lineage / conjuratio) +0.043 legitimate; guohui (national assembly, "hui" lineage) +0.015 legitimate; xuehui ("hui" lineage) −0.041 near neutral; Baoguohui ("hui" lineage) +0.030 legitimate; zhengdang (political party, "dang" lineage / universitas) −0.077 prohibited; mindang (people's party, "dang" lineage) −0.103 prohibited; geming dang (revolutionary party) +0.070 anomalously positive; weixin dang (reform party) +0.013 near-neutral; xian dang (constitutional party) +0.063 anomalously positive.

This contrast shows the factional prohibition did not block all imports of Western discourse—it precisely blocked only the "dang" lineage roots. "Hui" lineage (conjuratio) passes, "dang" lineage (universitas) is blocked—the last manifestation of the prohibition's precise morphology under Western impact.

Corpus-level critique of "zhengdang." "Zhengdang" appears only 6 times in the late Qing corpus (23.3 M characters). This sparsity itself is critical evidence—in imperial official discourse, "zhengdang" hardly entered any legitimate use context.

The anomaly of "geming dang" and "xian dang." These two have slightly positive late-Qing polarity (+0.070, +0.063), seemingly contradicting the claim. On closer look: sample sizes are small (n < 30), and their nearest neighbors are not legitimate semantic fields but rather "huidang" (secret society), "luandang" (rebel party), "luan" (chaos)—a compound phenomenon where "the object of attack simultaneously gains reverse support from sympathizers." See online appendix B for contextual analysis. The anomaly does not shake the core claim; it only illustrates the complexity of discursive warfare.

This is not a Hofstadter-style transition. The Hofstadter (1969) transition of American "party" from illegitimate to legitimate in 1780–1840 required all party vocabulary to legitimate simultaneously. The late-Qing situation has only "hui" lineage legitimating while "dang" lineage remains prohibited—a partial transition where the core universitas category does not legitimate. The eventual transition—Republican founding (1912), party system, party-state regime—occurred outside the imperial discursive framework, in Republican-era journalism and May Fourth thought after 1911.

The late-Qing failure was not for lack of new words. "Yihui" had legitimated; "guohui" too—both gained positive polarity in word-embedding space. What failed is universitas as legitimate political subject—"zhengdang" never achieved legitimacy within the imperial discursive framework. Until the Qing collapse in 1911, "dang" lineage roots remained in prohibited territory.

5. Cross-Dynasty Persistence: Discursive Dynamics

§4's findings summarize as: Chinese political vocabulary does not lack collective vocabulary, but precisely lacks the universitas category. Across 2,500 years, this absence operated with striking stability, crossing multiple foreign conquests and major institutional ruptures. Empirical evidence can tell us only "what happened," not "why it persisted so long." This chapter addresses the latter.

Standard institutional-change theory (North 1990; Greif 2006; North, Wallis & Weingast 2009; Acemoglu & Robinson 2012) is insufficient here. These theories emphasize "self-erosion"—every institution contains the seeds of its own change. But universitas absence has no documented critical junctures across 2,500 years, crossing multiple foreign conquests and major institutional ruptures, and even the post-1840 Western impact could not reform it within the imperial framework. The character of this persistence differs from "self-eroding" institutional path dependence.

5.1 Two Parallel Failures: Northern Song 1043 and Late Ming 1620s

The most direct evidence comes from two discursive wars 580 years apart, using the same discursive weapon, facing the same kind of opponent, ending in the same manner.

Northern Song Qingli Reform: 1043. Fan Zhongyan, Fu Bi, Ouyang Xiu and others, supported by Emperor Renzong, pushed for reform—accused by opponents of being "pengdang." Ouyang Xiu wrote On Factions (Pengdang Lun) in 1044—a key text in the history of Chinese political thought. The argument's strategy is lexical splitting: "Gentlemen ally with gentlemen through shared Way as friends; petty men ally with petty men through shared interest as faction… But I say petty men have no friendship; only gentlemen do." Through this split, "gentlemen's friendship" gains legitimacy, "petty men's faction" is defined as illegitimate.

Why did the argument fail? Three reasons. (i) Discursive inconsistency—Ouyang Xiu had to use the dichotomy of "false friendship / true friendship," which itself presupposes the existence of "pengdang" as a continuous negative category. (ii) Political ineffectiveness—On Factions was a counter-attack, not initiative, doing discursive self-defense from a defensive position, unable to reset the rules. (iii) Historical examples can be reversed—Ouyang Xiu's positive examples of Shun and King Wu can be turned around: "We must prohibit factions to avoid Han-Tang collapse." The Qingli reform was rejected by late 1044; Fan, Fu, and Ouyang were forced out.

Late Ming Donglin: 1620s. Facing eunuch-faction accusations, Donglin scholars again invoked Ouyang Xiu's On Factions. Ono Kazuko's Studies on Late Ming Factions and Societies explicitly records Gu Xiancheng and Gao Panlong reusing the "gentlemen's friendship / petty men's faction" dichotomy in political disputation—they used the same discursive weapon as 580 years before. The result is identical. In 1625 the Six Gentlemen of Donglin died in prison; in 1626 the Latter Seven Gentlemen followed. Ruan Dacheng's Donglin Roster mapping 109 Donglin members onto 108 Water Margin heroes completes the most thorough symbolic reversal of the "gentlemen's friendship" narrative.

580 years, the same discursive weapon, the same kind of opponent, the same failure—not coincidence. This shows universitas absence is not a one-off discursive event but a structural constraint, recurring before every generation of reformers, defeating the same discursive weapon in the same way every time.

5.2 Kawakatsu's Reverse Narrative: The Simultaneity of Loser and Winner

The first mechanism comes from an unexpected direction: placing the prohibition's losers and winners on the same map.

Kawakatsu Yoshio's Studies on the Six-Dynasties Aristocratic Society (1982) traces the fate of the Dangguzhihuo's "pure-stream" scholars at the Wei-Jin transition. Suppressed pure-stream notables—the Yingchuan Xun, Zhong, and Chen lineages, the Kong Rong-Zheng Xuan blocs—formed networks through teacher-student, disciple, and former-subordinate relations; their descendants became the new ruling class at the Cao Wei–Western Jin transition; the "central evaluation" of the Nine-Rank system was built precisely on their "pure discussion" tradition. The Dangguzhihuo's losers became, decades later, the winners of the aristocratic system.

Placing Kawakatsu's claim alongside this paper's finding: this paper argues universitas absence persists 2,500 years—a negative narrative about semantic-layer failure; Kawakatsu argues pure-stream transformed into aristocratic system after the prohibition—a positive narrative about social-layer success. Both narratives describe the same people, the same event, only seen at two different layers with different results.

This observation answers a deep question: why doesn't universitas absence self-erode? Standard institutional-change theory's "self-erosion" mechanism depends on a premise: the institution must produce concrete losers who have motivation to dismantle it. But universitas absence does not produce pure losers. Those labeled "X dang" lose at the discursive layer but often win at the social layer—pure-stream notables become aristocracy; Donglin lecturers' families become Jiangnan gentry; even late-Qing "reform party" members later become important Republican-era political figures. The simultaneity of loss and gain leaves the prohibition's "losers" without motivation to dismantle.

Going deeper: it is precisely this simultaneity that allows the prohibition to be repeatedly used by all political groups without anyone resisting. In every political conflict, the attacker uses "pengdang" to accuse opponents; the accused suffers discursively but their social position, lineage strength, and cultural capital are not destroyed by the accusation—they persist through other channels. In the next conflict, the previously accused may become the attacker, again using "pengdang" against new opponents. This eternal rotation turns the prohibition into a tool all political participants use repeatedly and none want dismantled.

In game-theoretic terms: the prohibition is a stable Nash equilibrium because no single party has an incentive to stop using it first. Everyone continues using is stable; any one abandoning first is unstable. This is why universitas absence can persist 2,500 years—its stability does not depend on any specific political structure but only on a simple game-theoretic fact.

5.3 De Weerdt on Southern Song Discursive Networks: Physical Existence is Not Sufficient

The second mechanism comes from an opposite empirical finding. Hilde De Weerdt's Information, Territory, and Networks (2015) systematically documents the trans-regional discursive network Southern Song literati built through notebooks, court gazettes, maps, collected works, private histories. The network's scale and density are not small—by the late 11th century, the Song court no longer monopolized information production about itself and its territory; literati circulated sensitive political commentary through unofficial texts.

This has direct implications for Mokyr-Tabellini. M-T's "buzz network" mechanism says the horizontal network of European guilds-cities-parliaments is key to constitutional evolution. If China had no such network, M-T's claim is verified in China. But De Weerdt breaks this logical chain—Southern Song already had a vast, trans-regional, multi-genre literati discursive network.

So why did Southern Song not produce constitutional results? De Weerdt's own answer is crucial: "The network strengthened the role of remonstrance to imperial power and expanded elite access to information, but did not create horizontal political structures or constitutional constraints." This is the further refinement of this paper's claim on De Weerdt's empirical basis: Mechanism 1b's physical existence is insufficient—even when a literati network exists, if it is framed at the discursive layer as "remonstrance to the emperor" rather than "independent political organization" (i.e., universitas), it cannot convert into constitutional effect. Southern Song literati could criticize the living in notebooks, but could not organize through "X dang" or "X hui" to form institutional constraints on imperial authority—because the discursive layer prohibited universitas.

5.4 Deng Xiaonan's "Ancestral Law": The Same-Shape Phenomenon of Discursive Instrumentalized Equilibrium

The third mechanism comes from a seemingly unrelated discursive field. Deng Xiaonan's The Law of the Ancestors (2006) systematically analyzes "ancestral law" as a Northern Song political discursive norm—a composite, stable in core spirit but flexible at the edges, essentially an aggregation of scholar-official discourse. Deng's key finding: "ancestral law" is invoked bidirectionally in factional strife. Fan Zhongyan's reform party used "ancestral law" as "theoretical basis for limiting current abuses and rectifying administrative customs"; only Wang Anshi dared "frontally question," declaring "ancestral law is not worth keeping." But more commonly both reformers and conservatives invoked "ancestral law."

Placing these two phenomena together, a common discursive dynamic emerges: as long as a political word can be bidirectionally invoked by the same group (usable for me, usable against me), it forms a stable instrumentalized equilibrium no one can dismantle. "Pengdang" is the negative version of this equilibrium—both sides use it to accuse the other, neither accepts the accusation; "ancestral law" is the positive version—both invoke it to legitimate themselves, neither can monopolize the legitimacy. This shows universitas absence is not an isolated "Chinese exceptionalism"—it is a manifestation of a specific discursive dynamic (bidirectional instrumentalized equilibrium). The same dynamic operates on other words.

5.5 Summary

Combining §5.1–5.4—Ouyang Xiu's On Factions's semantic inconsistency in two failures (5.1), Kawakatsu's reverse-narrative simultaneity of loser and winner (5.2), De Weerdt on insufficiency of physical network presence (5.3), Deng Xiaonan on the same-shape phenomenon of "ancestral law" as instrumentalized equilibrium (5.4)—the specific dynamics of universitas absence's 2,500-year persistence emerge.

It is not a particular dynasty's political suppression, not imperial authority's final veto on horizontal association, not a Confucian-ethics byproduct, not a linguistic expression of lineage structure. It is a specific, precisely identifiable discursive dynamic—bidirectional instrumentalized equilibrium—repeatedly used and thereby reinforced by all political participants, with no one motivated to dismantle because it produces no pure losers, and stable because the discursive-layer failure can be compensated at the social layer.

This dynamic makes universitas absence a phenomenon of a different character from organizational-institutional path dependence. §6 places this finding in the cross-civilizational comparative landscape.

6. Cross-Civilizational Comparison

6.1 Joseon Korea: Sarim Academies as Isolation Test

If universitas absence is only a special product of Chinese political culture or Chinese state structure, it should weaken or disappear in other societies operating under Confucian-classical discourse. Joseon Korea (1392–1897) provides the cleanest isolation test.

Joseon was actually more complete than Northern Song on Mechanism 1. Academies, starting with Yi Hwang's founding of the Sosu Academy in 1543, grew to about 1,000 by 1741. By Mokyr-Tabellini standards they are textbook territorial corporations: endowed land, tax exemption, royal grants of rice and slaves, autonomous rules, multi-generational scholar genealogies (Ch'oe 1999). They provided 200+ years of rural infrastructure for sarim political-faction reproduction.

If Mechanism 1 alone suffices to trigger constitutional evolution, Joseon should have been the most likely candidate. Yet Joseon produced no constitutional result. In 1741 King Yeongjo explicitly framed academy disbandment as "ending 붕당 (pengdang) corruption"—using the same two Chinese characters "朋黨" debated by Northern Song reformers and opponents during 1043–1100. The Confucian discursive grid travels with classical vocabulary across the Sino-Korean cultural sphere, remaining effective across polity boundaries.

Joseon's failure can be read from three mutually compatible angles. (a) Mechanism 2 internal violation: Joseon's academy network was infrastructure for factional segregation, with Easterner and Westerner networks producing mutually antagonistic discourse. (b) Mechanism 1c violation: Yeongjo's 1741 abolition preemptively eliminated institutional balance before constitutional crystallization, structurally parallel to Meiji 1871's haihan chiken (abolition of domains). (c) Mechanism 2 root: the prohibition's discursive constraint operates wherever Confucian classical vocabulary norms operate, independent of organizational substrate—the constraint follows discursive vocabulary, not polity boundaries.

The current empirical basis on Joseon rests on secondary literature (Ch'oe 1999). The next research stage should train an independent corpus of the Joseon Veritable Records and perform cross-dynasty word-embedding analysis on the Korean "붕당"—elevating Joseon from a qualitative comparative case to a two-case quantitative test.

6.2 Medieval Genoa: The Fragility of Path B

If Joseon shows Mechanism 1 alone is insufficient, Greif's (2006) Genoese case shows the opposite possibility: Mechanism 1 is not strictly necessary either—provided sufficient institutional self-enforcing design.

Medieval Genoa (1099–1339) was governed by consanguineous lineages (alberghi), structurally closer to Chinese lineages than to European territorial guilds. Yet the podesteria (1194–1339) achieved 145 years of commercial-revolution stability through ingenious mechanism design. The core is the podestà institution: the chief executive must be a non-Genoese, employed for one year without re-election, with his own armed retinue—forming a delicate three-way balance among the Maneciano lineage, the Carmadino lineage, and the podestà. Long-distance trade grew at over 6% annually in 1191–1214; by 1314 it reached forty-six times the 1160 level.

But this alternative path is fragile. Greif himself argues for the self-erosion of the podesteria—it preserved and reinforced the underlying lineage structure rather than dissolving it. After 1339, Genoa collapsed into 200+ years of civil war, with thirty-nine documented revolutions (Epstein 1996), finally losing to Venice in 1381 and entering long decline. The fragility's deep root is in Mechanism 3: the underlying community was not dissolved.

The Genoa case illuminates imperial China by structural analogy. China had institutional structures similar to podestà—the eunuch system. Eunuchs were similarly non-aristocratic insiders, similarly possessing personal coercive capacity, similarly independent of scholar-official lineage networks. But the eunuch system's function differed fundamentally from podestà: it served imperial sovereignty, not cross-lineage balance. Why? Combining Greif's framework with this paper's §4 empirical results: the Confucian discursive grid forbids "balance among elite coalitions" from being conceptualized as a legitimate political problem solvable by institutional design. At the discursive layer, "factional balance" is already a priori illegitimate (any "X dang" is already prohibited). Imperial China lacked the podestà institution not because the design was unknown—it was fully reachable in institutional imagination—but because the political logic generating Path B never crystallized as legitimate.

6.3 The Islamic World: An Isomorphic Companion Case

A key question now forms: is universitas absence China-specific or a specific case of a more general cross-civilizational dynamic? Answering requires finding a mechanism-level isomorphic case in a different civilizational context.

Jared Rubin's Rulers, Religion, and Wealth (2017) provides such a case. Rubin argues the Islamic world experienced a discursive-legitimacy prohibition near-isomorphic to universitas absence at the mechanism level—the ulama (religious scholars) provided legitimacy to sultans in exchange for a seat at the bargaining table, excluding merchant elites from this coalition. This arrangement self-reinforces: once a sultan depends on ulama legitimation, turning to other legitimacy sources would offend the entrenched ulama.

Structural isomorphism: both feature discursive-layer prohibition (of universitas in China; of merchant access to legitimacy bargaining in Islam), self-reinforcing equilibrium, excluded elites (cross-kinship horizontal political organization; commercial elites), production of no pure losers, and cross-polity stability (pre-Qin to late Qing; Abbasid to Ottoman). Three implications: (i) universitas absence is not China-unique but a specific form of a particular discursive dynamic; (ii) "discursive-legitimacy prohibition" as an institutional form parallel to "institutional-organizational path dependence" should be included in comparative political science's core theoretical toolbox; (iii) this paper's method (word-embedding analysis tracking 2,500 years of discursive prohibition) can be replicated on Arabic-language corpora to quantitatively verify Rubin's qualitative claim.

6.4 Singapore: A Paradigm of Simultaneous Three-Mechanism Reform

Singapore (independent 1965 to present) is a Chinese-majority polity (about 75% Chinese), having systematically reformed all three mechanisms.

Mechanism 1 reform: Westminster parliament established as the supreme-law-counterparty institution. Numerous statutory boards built—Economic Development Board (EDB), Housing Development Board (HDB), Central Provident Fund (CPF), etc.—each with independent legal personality, independent budget, independent operating rules. They are not lineage extensions but real territorial corporations.

Mechanism 2 reform: the English public sphere bypasses universitas absence. Although Singapore's official languages include English, Mandarin, Malay, and Tamil, important political topics are discussed in English. Parliamentary debate, legal texts, administrative documents, news commentary are primarily in English. This choice is not accidental—it lets Singapore's public political discussion bypass the Chinese-vocabulary semantic trap. "Political party" in English has no "pengdang" stigma. Through language switching, Singapore effectively exited the universitas-absence discursive framework.

Mechanism 3 reform: HDB policy deliberately dismantles consanguineous community. Since the 1960s, large-scale public housing has been built—about 80% of Singaporean citizens today live in HDB flats. Specific policies include the Ethnic Integration Policy 1989 (racial quotas), small nuclear-family units, and mandatory SERS (Selective En bloc Redevelopment Scheme). This is the concrete plan for Ōtsuka-sense kyōdōtai dissolution.

Singapore's success is not the success of any single mechanism but the result of simultaneously reforming all three. Any partial reform targeting only one mechanism would be insufficient.

7. Lock Persistence Dynamics: Dialogue with Institutional-Change Theory

7.1 Limits of Standard Institutional-Change Theory

Contemporary institutional-change theory's mainstream—North (1990), North & Weingast (1989), Greif (2006), Acemoglu & Robinson (2012), North, Wallis & Weingast (2009)—shares a core claim: all institutions contain the seeds of their own change. Each institutional use produces new pressures—concrete winners and losers, new resource allocation, new discursive-legitimation challenges—which accumulate over time and eventually trigger institutional change at "critical junctures."

But universitas absence rejects this framework. It spans 2,500 years without any documented critical juncture, crosses multiple foreign conquests and major institutional ruptures, and even the post-1840 Western impact could not reform it within the imperial framework. The depth of this persistence is puzzling.

7.2 Universitas Absence as a Different Kind of Institutional Form

The paper's core theoretical claim: universitas absence differs in nature from organizational-institutional path dependence; it is a different kind of institutional form.

(i) It produces no concrete losers. Material institutions produce concrete winners and losers; losers can mobilize forces for change. But discursive-legitimacy prohibition produces no concrete losers—every political actor can accuse opponents of "pengdang" while denying it for himself. The lock is therefore universally instrumentalized rather than universally resisted. Every factional struggle uses the prohibition to attack opponents; every use reinforces the prohibition.

(ii) It mutually reinforces with co-symbiotic structures. Mechanism 2 and Mechanism 3 mutually reinforce in the Chinese context. The Confucian "Five Relationships" (ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger, friend-friend) precisely depict the ethics of consanguineous community; consanguineous community in turn provides empirical bedrock for Confucian ethics. The two mechanisms form a stable coupling equilibrium.

(iii) External shocks are absorbed within compatible discursive frameworks. Foreign conquest (Mongol, Manchu) made Chinese society face bureaucratic systems organized by principles compatible with the prohibition. Post-1840 Western impact brought alternative frameworks, but late-imperial responses were either suppressed or absorbed. The real transformation—Republican-era party politics, May Fourth discursive revolution, post-1949 party-state regime—all occurred outside the imperial discursive framework.

7.3 Two Historical Paths for Reforming the Prohibition

Path A: wholesale exit from the discursive framework. Singapore took this path through the English public sphere. The condition for success: there must be an already-developed alternative language system as exit option.

Path B: state-led discursive engineering. The post-1949 Chinese Communist Party, through top-down party-state authority, pulled "dang" out of its negative semantic field, imposing new positive semantic associations. This reform was gradually completed in the 20th century 1950s–80s. But the success is point-like, not structural—"dang" as abbreviation for "Chinese Communist Party" gained positive polarity, but other L7b vocabulary ("paixi" factions, "bangpai" cliques) remains negative. The success of discursive engineering is monopolistic—it remolded the prohibition into a new form of "one party legitimate, all others illegitimate."

7.4 Methodological Implications for Institutional-Change Theory

First, discursive-legitimacy prohibition needs to be included in comparative political science's core theoretical toolbox—it is an independent institutional form, not merely "cultural background" for existing institutional variables. Second, analyzing such prohibitions requires new methodological tools—word-embedding analysis, network analysis, concept-drift analysis. Third, the time scale needs recalibration—discursive-legitimacy prohibitions may operate on a time scale an order of magnitude longer than organizational-institutional path dependence. Fourth, "self-erosion" as universal mechanism needs to be qualified—all organizational-institutional forms producing concrete losers contain the seeds of their own change, but discursive-legitimacy prohibitions produce no concrete losers.

8. Limits and Future Directions

8.1 Methodological Limits

The boundary of static word2vec. §3.5 addresses this through rule-based polysemy disambiguation. A more thorough approach is contextualized word embeddings—BERT-based GuwenBERT, SikuBERT, etc. This paper has not yet done this comparison; it should be a priority for the next stage.

Corpus selection bias. The late-Qing corpus is dominated by the Qing Veritable Records, a state-style that systematically suppresses heterodox vocabulary. §4.11 already acknowledged this and limited the claim to "within the imperial-official framework." Future work should incorporate Republican-era newspaper corpora, revolutionary manifesto corpora, treaty-port bilingual printed matter.

Anchor-word selection risk. This paper uses four independent anchor families + extended 30-character anchor set + leave-one-out cross-validation + internal cohesion check, four robustness strategies. A more thorough approach uses unsupervised methods (PCA / UMAP reduction).

Endogeneity. Causal direction between discourse and institutions has recursive dynamics—the lock is reproduced by every generation of users while constraining their discursive options. §4.11 provides empirical evidence that discourse precedes institutions through the late-Qing polarity of "zhengdang" preceding the actual party institution.

8.2 Empirical Extensions

Four next-stage tasks: (i) train an independent Joseon Veritable Records corpus, elevating §6.1's isolation test from qualitative to quantitative; (ii) extend to Vietnamese corpora—Vietnamese Later Lê–Nguyễn is another Confucian-discursive-grid case; (iii) BERT contextualized-vector robustness check—redo the Donglin naming experiment on Late Ming corpus; (iv) connect with Jin Guantao's DSMCTL (1830–1930)—integrating this paper's pre-1911 quantitative findings with Republican-era analysis, forming a continuous narrative from pre-Qin to Republican.

8.3 Cross-Civilizational Extensions

Replication on Islamic corpora. This paper's method can be replicated on classical Arabic corpora to quantitatively verify Rubin's qualitative claim. European control experiments. The Hofstadter (1969) American "party" transition from illegitimate to legitimate in 1780–1840 can be quantitatively replicated using EEBO-ECCO and other English early-modern corpora; Greif's claims on Genoa can be tested using Latin chronicle corpora.

9. Conclusion

Back to the opening scene. In 1894 Sun Yat-sen chose "Xingzhonghui" not "Xingzhongdang." In 1898 the Guangxu Emperor's "Mingding Guoshi" edict contains no "zhengdang." In 1905 Chinese students in Tokyo used "Tongmenghui" not "Tongmengdang." In 1912 Song Jiaoren reorganized the Tongmenghui into the Kuomintang, having to use "guo" (national) to lend legitimacy to "dang"; this experiment collapsed when Song was assassinated in 1913.

Behind every detail is the same question: why is "hui" legitimate while "dang" remains an attack-word? Why does every reformer trying to organize a supra-kinship, durable political body capable of dialogue with supreme authority have to walk carefully around the character "dang"?

This paper's answer comes in four specific findings.

(i) Chinese political vocabulary does not lack "collective vocabulary"—L1 friendship, L2 moral identity, L3 ethical principles, L4 scholarly activity, L5 Conjuratio, L7a Communitas are all legitimate. What is precisely prohibited is L7b Universitas—dang, qun, tu, bei, lei, chai, zhong and other candidate roots.

(ii) The sole escape is "hui" (μ = +0.049). It bears nearly all legitimate horizontal-association functions—yihui, guohui, xuehui, Baoguohui, Xingzhonghui, Tongmenghui, Guangfuhui. This is no accident—"hui"'s semantic core is contractual, visible, members-preserved collective (i.e., conjuratio), not absorbed-individual legitimate collective (i.e., universitas).

(iii) The prohibition operates at the semantic-structure layer of "acting as legitimate political subject," not at the specific-character layer. Single "she" and "meng" are legitimate, but "Fushe" and "jiemeng" are stigmatized; "pai" is near-neutral but "xuepai" sinks in late Qing. Reforming the prohibition cannot be achieved by creating a new character—once the new character is identified as carrying the universitas semantic structure, it is automatically pulled into prohibited territory.

(iv) The late-Qing dual track provides the cleanest modern verification. "Hui" lineage (yihui, guohui, xuehui, Baoguohui) legitimating, "dang" lineage (zhengdang, mindang) blocked—the last manifestation of the prohibition's precise morphology under Western impact.

China did not develop constitutionalism not because Chinese discourse lacks "collective vocabulary"—it precisely lacks a specific category: universitas as legitimate political subject. From the pre-Qin Analects' "qun er bu dang" onward, across the judicial institutionalization of the Han partisan prohibition, the Northern Song Qingli discursive war, the Late Ming Donglin naming war, the late-Qing introduction of "zhengdang"—2,500 years of stable operation.

Integrating into a three-mechanism framework—territorial corporate organization, discursive legitimacy of horizontal association, dissolution of consanguineous community—this constitutes the Chinese-context refinement of the Acemoglu & Robinson "narrow corridor" model. Joseon (Mechanism 1 sufficient but 2, 3 failed), Genoa (Mechanism 1 absent but partial substitution via Path B, eventual self-erosion), Islamic world (mechanism-level isomorphic with China), Singapore (simultaneous three-mechanism reform)—four cross-civilizational cases verify this framework from different angles.

This paper contributes to three literatures. To computational conceptual history: it demonstrates that quantitative cross-dynasty vocabulary analysis can produce results unattainable by close reading alone—specifically, the precise boundary map of "legitimate categories / prohibited categories" produced by scanning the full spectrum rather than single characters. To Chinese political history: it refines Levine's "factional lock" hypothesis into "the precise absence of the Universitas category," placing "dang" research back into the entire semantic field it inhabits. To comparative institutional analysis: it provides the first cross-civilizational quantitative test of the Confucian discursive-legitimacy prohibition, placing it alongside Rubin's Islamic religious-legitimacy prohibition to show that "discursive-legitimacy prohibition" is an institutional form different in nature from organizational-institutional path dependence.

Back to Sun Yat-sen's choice of "Xingzhonghui" in 1894. This choice is neither accidental linguistic sense nor any individual's strategy—it is the concrete result of 2,500 years of discursive sediment landing on one specific reformer. Behind "hui" is a complete legitimate conjuratio semantic field—meng, shi, qi, yue, jieyi, huimeng. Behind "dang" is the judicial-persecution semantic field of the Han partisan prohibition, the failed echo of Ouyang Xiu's On Factions, the concrete blood of the Six Gentlemen of Donglin dying in prison, the accumulated pressure of "X dang" as unified illegitimate semantic cluster. Sun Yat-sen choosing "hui" not "dang" is the same thing as Ouyang Xiu in 1044 choosing "gentlemen's friendship" not "petty men's faction," as Donglin lecturers in the 1620s choosing "Shuyuan" not "dang," as the Fushe in 1629 choosing "she" not "dang"—each time a specific reformer's specific response to the same historical pressure, each time following the same discursive ecology: pick words from legitimate conjuratio / communitas, avoid the prohibited universitas.

The specific form of Universitas absence is here. It is not abstract "Chinese exceptionalism," not generic "Chinese spirit," not any particular dynasty's political suppression. It is a specific, precisely identifiable discursive dynamic—the precise prohibition of a particular semantic structure—operating repeatedly across multiple polities for 2,500 years. Understanding it is the necessary starting point for understanding Chinese political evolution; reforming it is the necessary starting point for understanding China's future evolution.
